Pull Yourself Together People sometimes say “pull yourself together”, often disparagingly, and it
never really made sense to me until recently. In my earlier work we explored the paradox of
nothingness and the idea of creating new parts of ourselves. In this essay we’ll explore the act
itself, and as you will see, the experience is somewhat like quite literally pulling yourself
together.

I usually begin with mindfulness and reflection. It allows me to observe my mind without shame or
judgement, a little like setting up a mirror before performing surgery on yourself. It allows me to
observe the motions of my mind as it follows its natural course. I watch and wait patiently for the
maladaptive patterns within me to slowly pull me away from peace towards chaos, and while it doesn’t
always happen the same way the effect is usually predictable. Inner calm is gradually replaced with
turmoil, and it build itself until it becomes obvious. If you’ve ever had frustrating thoughts you
know it.

After finding the parts of me which aren’t working, my next step is to gently get a handle on them.
It’s not about fighting them or arguing with yourself, it’s about leaning into them with genuine
self compassion and curiosity, as if trying to help vines grow through l rubble. It takes curiosity
to find the damaged parts, and defiance to push through the defences and reach them. Keep your focus
on connecting with yourself, like reaching out to a scared child within you.

As you push ahead notice how the resistance builds, and rather than letting the resistance turn you
away, push head on into it. Let the resistance be your guide, and work to find more of it. We’re
searching for the broken parts of ourselves, and they’re pushing us away, so we can follow that
resistance to get to their root.

It’s here we begin to experience the pulling yourself together. As you get nearer to the core of
your injured self, you will begin to experience something that’s difficult to put into words, but
let’s try anyway. In essence there is a part of you which has detached, hidden itself away, and is
operating somewhat autonomously. Were kelt away by defence mechanisms, and the closer we get the
more integrated it becomes. It’s this reintegrating of damaged pieces which creates the feeling of
pulling yourself together, and once you learn to ignore the pain of the defence mechanisms it’s
actually quite pleasurable.

When we first learn to challenge our defences it’s a brutal fight for survival. Despair and worry
are our companions, with confusion and dissociation as our masters. Even in this state though, there
is always a part of us that wants to keep going and find inner peace. Reach for that gentle spark,
nurture it, and in time it will become a great fire burning through the trauma of the past. Find
your passion for healing and growing, and everything will fall into place. As we learn to love the
pain of growth, the feeling of desperation will be replaced with a deep satisfaction, somewhat like
a mental equivalent of the burn you feel after a workout. No longer are we fighting ourselves, we
are fighting for ourselves. Our enemy becomes the maladaptive behaviours we acquired to survive, and
our defences lose their power as we learn to separate them from ourselves. As you push through your
defences with compassion for yourself and a take-no-prisoners attitude towards the abusive masters
they once served, a deep sense of satisfaction and self-respect surfaces. Enjoy it, it’s the feel
trauma losing its hold.

Behind the defences you’ll find a part of you that wants or needs something it was never allowed to
have. It could be violent, aggressive, disgusting, nonsensical, or just plain silly. Such are the
interests of most children. It can sometimes be wise to withholding it, in a literal sense,
especially if it would hurt yourself or others, but if you dig deep enough and understand the why
behind the request, you’ll find something undeniably human and sensible. It’s here our opportunity
for repair presents itself. Pulling yourself together requires giving up your adult convictions and
letting that part of you have what it wants. Our defences and our trauma are damage to our
relationship with ourselves, and repair begins by putting down righteousness and allowing space for
humanity. Let the trapped part of you be free by giving it what it wants and needs. Understanding,
validation, and acceptance. Let it have what it wants.

A little anecdote from my past might help here. I once wrote an article on freedom which was meant
as nothing more than a simple meditation of the question of how to be genuinely free. The draft
flowed from my heart and filled the page effortlessly. I was proud of what I wrote, and wanted to
share it with the world. In time though, I found myself nipping snd tucking at the text until both
my message and voice had been distorted beyond recognition. I was stuck between wanting to publish
so I could move on and do something else, and feeling the need to make my work perfect. I meditated
on this, burned through the defences, and found the part of me which was asking over and over again:
Why can’t I just publish my work? Why does it have to be prefect?

Letting this part of me come to the surface revealed a memory. A time many years ago, or perhaps
many times all blurred into one, where my parents had distorted my schoolwork for their own goals. I
remember once sitting beside my mother, cold and uncomfortable, as she endlessly nipped and tucked
at a simple fourth grade essay until it was devoid of my original voice and passion. It retained the
same meaning and went on to receive an A, but the words were subtly pandering to an invisible
audience of critics. I realise now it was her own trauma and inner critic running the show, but the
nuance of intergenerational trauma is a topic for another day.

In this memory, all I wanted to leave well enough alone, have a hug, and go to bed. In the present
moment, all I wanted to was to publish my essay and move on. Endless revisions were wearing me down
and reinforcing the traumatic idea that my work needs to be perfect. Pulling myself together here
meant pushing against the need for perfection and just publishing. It’s what I ended up doing, and
the cold/uncomfortable child within me is no longer trapped there. We’re one now.

I want to leave you with an exercise. Today as you go about your day, I want you to find one thing
you never let yourself have. It could be a coffee after 12, it could be an early end to the work
day, or it could be a snack that pushes you past your caloric limit. I want you to find the part of
you that wants this, and push through the defences with passion and resilience. Enjoy the feeling of
burning through your trauma and stop when you find something understandably human. Give it what it
really wants (not the surface request it for coffee etc) and see how it feels. I suspect you will
feel more whole.

At Some Point You Have to Get Out and Push Our world runs on automation, and there's more around you
than you may realise. It’s not all about robots and self-driving cars, there are tiny programs in
virtually everything complex now. From phones to microwaves to trains, software runs our lives.
We’re going to talk about a different kind of software though, we’re going to talk about human
software. Specifically, the distributed system we call culture. If we look at people as parts of a
giant computer, distributed across time and space, it becomes obvious that culture is a program we
all run. We pass it on to our children, and we interact with others through it. Cultures interact,
shift, mutate, and sometimes go extinct. In some way, culture is a vehicle for humanity, embedded
within us but also carrying us across the ages, binding us through adversity and prosperity alike.
Like all machines though it has no free will, no inherent ability to grow beyond its limits, and
only a limited capacity for self-repair. Where this all leads is the need to occasionally get out
and push.

When the machine breaks down and all that remains are the users, the people, then we become the
redundancy and the fallback. We can set up machines that work for a very long time without
maintenance, but they will slowly wear down and need repair. Until we find a way to breathe true
life into our systems, to give them a way to transcend their limitations, we will never escape our
role as the last line of defence against entropy.

This is all to say that every machine, every automation, and every system eventually runs down and
needs to be fixed. We are the builders and we are the fixers. Perhaps we are not immune though, and
perhaps we too will run down one day. This leads to one question.

When humanity breaks, who gets out to push?

I wonder if being able to ask the question brings with it some responsibility. Do we, the question
smiths and the answer seekers, bear unique responsibility here? Do we as inquisitive and curious
minds inherit the responsibility? Perhaps, perhaps not. Opinions will differ and views will change
over time. I can only speak for myself.

Maybe it’s not a question of whether we have responsibility, but opportunity and choice. We can
always allow things to break, to wear down, and then let them go. We can decide we have had enough
and choose to get off the ride of eternal failure and repair. It’s a choice though, and one only you
can make. Until you yield control to others, no one can make your choices for you, of that I am
certain.

Meditation on Life, Death, Time and Identity A friend once asked me what would I do if I knew I was
going to die soon. At the time the question stumped me, and was an invitation to think clearly about
how I want to use my time. In recent months though I’ve come to understand the question was flawed.
It implies for a moment that I am not going to die soon, that my life would be somehow different if
I were going to die, and that I would make different choices.

Having been through therapy, three sessions a week, my perspective on life is now radically
different. It was perhaps one day during the pandemic when this question was most on my mind. I am
not sure how it happened. Or what prompted it, but I found myself sitting in a dark closet holding
myself. I have always been aware that I am going to die, but on this occasion the knowledge wasn’t
buried or repressed. It was front and center, and it was real.

I am going to die eventually. So are you. We all are. It’s not a matter of question or even
perspective, it’s a simple fact. No matter how much we try to avoid it, death is coming for all of
us. Everyone you ever met, loved, hated, sired, kissed, or walked past. Every boss, every coworker,
and every client. From the moment we are born we are on an inescapable path towards our final
destination, the cessation of all biological functions.

When this thought first came into my consciousness it was frightening, harrowing really, and I
locked myself in a cupboard to handle the terror. I don’t want to die, I remember thinking. Death
seemed like a vice slowly closing in. Of course in hindsight this was probably a panic attack, or
some form of existential crisis. Perhaps to put it another way, reality was creeping through my
defense.

As the years have gone by, and as the sands have slipped through the hourglass, my fear and terror
have been replaced by something else, a gentle acceptance. Yes, I am going to die, but it’s not
terrible. I’ve been dying my whole life, and the final moment comes right at the end. This means
worrying about it, and concerning myself with the oblivion that most likely awaits me, is a cruel
joke. We don’t need to worry about death because in a way, it never really comes to pass.

What I mean is death is the end. The last frame in the movie, so to speak. Once it happens there is
no more time to experience, no more memories to form, and no way to be. To spend the life we have
fixated and focused on that is to give up the only time we have alive. In a sense, using a life to
worry about death is a form of death in and of itself. Premature death by virtue of life wasted on
fear.

I’m not sure if this makes any sense. What I mean to say is. Between now and your inevitable death
there are countless things you could do. Choosing to spend your time worried about losing them in
your inevitable death only draws out the suffering. Instead, just let it be. Allow death to be the
final frame in the movie, and spend the time you have doing something else. Giving it concern only
takes away from the time you have to live.

This is different from rejection, from avoidance, and from striving for change. When we choose to
push the knowledge of death out of our minds, we end up giving it more strength. The effort spent
denying something is effort not spent being present to reality, and it often doesn’t work. Knowledge
of your impending doom will show up somewhere else, and it will never truly disappear. Instead, just
be at peace with it. Let it be.

Freedom from death is not eternal life, it’s acceptance of mortality. It’s gentle awareness of
fragility and gratitude for each day. Acceptance means knowing death is the end, and choosing to
focus on what’s important.

Don’t fear death. Don’t fear life. They are two sides of the same coin. Just let death be, and one
day when it arrives, you won’t even know. That's the beauty of death.

If we are to give time to death, let it be restful. Let us accept death as a release for burden and
a gift that washes away pain. Perhaps death is the universe allowing an exit, and giving us a limit
to suffering. Being immortal, involuntarily, is perhaps the worst form of torture one could ever
comprehend.

I wonder, is death the end, or the beginning? To bacteria death is surely the end, reduction to mere
molecules with no life of their own, and yet what are we on a cosmic scale if not bacteria
desperately clinging to the surface of a rock flying through space.

Our lives feel long, but to anyone on their death bed I’m sure the opposite is true. This reveals a
funny illusion of time. The moment feels real, but the moment is never solid. It’s ever passing,
ever changing, endlessly moving on to the next scene in the play of life. The fire feels infinitely
far away until it arrives, and the past feels both long and short. Paradoxical, but sensible. Time,
or rather our perception of it, is an illusion. We are not moving through time, time is moving
through us. We are still, the world is not.

Every moment that has ever existed for you felt real. Felt present. Felt like now. Yet only the one
you live in feels real now. How can it be that time passes. How can it be that we travel along a
stream with no means of control? Adrift like leaves. Perhaps we exist at all points in our lives,
and the moment is an illusion. Or perhaps we do move through time. It’s hard to understand. How can
the moment be real, then slip away?

I believe the answer is simple. The moment doesn’t leave. This moment is the same one as has always
been here. Beyond just a definition and a semantic difference, perhaps the moment is like a
projector screen. Blank and open to whatever images come to pass. We are born, view the moving
images, and learn that we are moving. We never stop to consider whether we have been sitting in the
same place all our lives.

Look around you. What do you see? I notice my dog, curled up on my lap and gently breathing in and
out. I notice my television, my refrigerator, and my couch. I notice the floor beneath my feet, and
the couch pressing against my body. Perhaps you’re in a similar setting, or perhaps you’re somewhere
radically different. It doesn’t matter.

One way to view this scene would be a moment that has come to pass. I moved from the past, as a leaf
in the stream, and came to rest in this place, albeit temporarily. I haven’t moved in space, and
there have been no outside events, so the environment is stable. I could stay here for many hours,
until of course the clock ticks down and it becomes time to eat food. Not to get off track though, I
digress.

In short, this moment contains these items in this configuration. I moved from another moment into
it, and eventually I will leave it for another moment. Perhaps though, we could view it differently.

I was born in this moment. I have lived in this moment my whole life. I will die in this moment.
Everything here, from the items around me to my beloved dog came into the moment and will eventually
leave, like pedestrians strolling past a shop window. We would never think to ask why the window
moved, why do we ask why the time passes.

Of course there’s a problem here. I am not simply a window. I have a body, a mind, perhaps even a
soul. These are not the moment, and they too are passing by the window. Who I am, from the neurons
to the psychological experience, came into this moment and will one day leave. Perhaps the paradox
of time is more deeply rooted in identity than any other.

Could it be possible that we are not people, humans, of even lives. Are we the moment itself? Are
our bodies merely biological systems that grow into the moment only to eventually blow away like
dust in a storm. Thoughts and feelings drift into the moment, entire minds form, and an ego takes
root. It clings to the idea that it is reality, that it is here, that it is me. Perhaps that is the
illusion.

If the moment is eternal, and the events of our lives are just a passing phenomena, if our minds and
souls are just paint on a canvas with such detail and beauty as to convince the canvas it itself is
the paint, what are we really?

Perhaps what we are is unknowable. Perhaps any attempt to know is to grasp at passing images.

There is no self. There is no other. There is no time. There js no space. There is no now. There is
no then. There is no future. There are no people. There is only nothing.

Endless nothing. Nothing within nothing. From that nothing comes the possibility of everything. The
seeds of life sprout conscious minds which ask questions and seek answers. Those minds form
identities, selves, and comprehend death. They seek to know what is beyond themselves, but forget
that they are not real.

We are just images on a canvas, so rich and detailed as to convince the canvas it has become alive.
But we are not real. We are not here. We are nothing. From nothing we are born and to nothing we
return.

Everything between us simply nothingness taking its course. So to ask what comes after death is to
lose sight of the truth. We were never real. We were just the canvas. We are just images. We are
clouds passing by. One day when we are gone, all that will remain is potential, the possibility of
new images, and a space for new minds to ask themselves the same questions all over again.

If you truly grasp this, then death is nothing to fear. You were never truly alive, so you cannot
truly die. Or perhaps, to put it another way, how can you be so sure you aren’t already dead?

This line of thought is far from complete. There are unanswered questions, and several paradoxes
that need to be untied. What system draws onto the canvas? Why does each frame follow from the last?
Is there anything beyond the canvas, what drives it? Is the canvas endless, or does it have a limit.

I sense there’s a deeper truth here yet to be uncovered. The answer will reveal itself eventually.
Perhaps not in this lifetime though, and I suspect any answer I find will only raise more questions.

Yes I like that. Managing my critic is a central piece of my personal philosophy. The Critical
Protector The critic lives within all of us. It’s the hand which gently guides us away from danger,
and it’s the voice which sees injustice and guides us towards a kinder world. It’s also the voice
which tears down cities, the fist that mutilates children, and the brittle tar which holds back
progress. The critic’s benevolence is matched only by its malice, and a critic twisted into a
protector is a force to be reckoned with. In my case the critic was running the show for a great
many years before I learned to disconnect and tame it.

In this moment as I write my critic nags at me. It insists I have nothing to say, it warns that my
contributions will be laughed at, and it endeavours to show me all the ways my work could be put to
misuse. That voice is not a friend, that voice is a malignant cancer which has no cure. A parasite.
A disease. The critic here holds me back not out of compassion but out of fear. This is the critical
protector.

The critical protector forms when children are subject to psychological and emotional trauma with no
hope of escape. It’s a natural adaptation, a necessary stage in survival, because it offers
sanctuary from the world. It offers a deep cave in which to hide, where no light may reach. Though
terrifying, such a place offers safety by shielding the child from words designed to cut into them.
Almost nothing can breach the critical protector, because it guards against everything. Every
sentence and every word is scrutinised, every syllable and inflection dissected, and every slight
intonation is evaluated and inspected under a microscope for signs of danger.

The cruel irony of the critical protector is the way it turns the child’s natural talents against
itself. A naturally sensitive child will become sensitive to the smallest criticism, and a naturally
assertive child will become agressive at the slightest hint of danger. The critical protector
believes, for reasons which are not alien, that anything less than perfect is dangerous.

If you are so fortunate to have never encountered such people or formed such a protector within
yourself then the people around you are truly kind. Perhaps though, there is still something you
could learn.

Before we get ahead of ourselves, let’s take a moment to center ourselves. The critical protector,
malevolent and hateful, is not always so powerful. In children of abusive homes it can be
particularly loud, but it exists to some degree in many others. Intolerant cultures, of which there
are many, can also product critical protectors by subtly and continually exposing their populations
to negative self images.

Beyond culture and upbringing, life itself presents challenges to even the most well founded and
supported individuals. The death of a beloved parent, spouse, child, pet, or other family member can
be profoundly difficult, and can sometimes trigger the growth of a critical protector. Other
examples include widespread economic downturn, losing a job, sheltering in place for months/years,
and random traumatic events. It’s just the nature of existence.

Life is a gift, but that gift springs in a hostile world, and to live is to walk through a minefield
with the knowledge that one day, no matter how well you step, you are going to die. That knowledge
lives within each of us, and it’s the role of the critical protector to keep it away from conscious
experience at all costs. To be aware of death at every moment would be anxiety inducing for many
people, so our critical protector comes between us and the truth. It has many weapons and tools at
its disposal, and we are going to systematically and methodically look at each.

If this is all sounding a little Jungian with archtypes and broad overstatment, that’s quite a valid
concern. I’m speaking broadly here, and there are outliers to my view. To briefly acknowledge them,
I am aware some cultures bring awareness to death each day and others actively suppress criticism in
favour of curiosity. These cultures are deserving of respect for their longstanding and deeply
ingrained methods for handling the critical protector. I’m also aware that not everyone fears death
and not everyone accepts death as an inevitability. I cannot disagree with how people feel, for
emotions are deeply personal, but I would like to remind any immortalists among my readers of the
impending and inevitable heat death of the universe.

We digress. Back to the topic at hand. In this chapter we will discuss:

1. The ways the critical protector shows up in our daily experiences.
1. The various tools and weapons the critical protector uses to blind us.
1. The means to separate ourselves from the critical protector and view the world differently.
1. The value of a free life.
1. The times when the critical protector is necessary.

My goal is to help you find a new way to view the world. Like a horse drawn to water though, I can
only show you the way. You must make the choice yourself and the effort required can only come from
you. Depending on how ingrained and controlling your critical protector is, you will find this
exercise anywhere between a light walk in the park to a soul wrenching exercise in personal
transformation.

We are all born judging. It’s in our nature and is a natural part of humanity. Judgement, by its
very nature, is a comparison between what we know to be true and what we see to be true. It’s an
approach to conflict which seeks to destroy and refine until only a pure truth remains. It should
come as nor surprise that it dominates society given how much support it lends to individual
pursuits and institutional stability. Judging helps us stay grounded in facts and reason. Yet it
also builds walls around our hearts and eventually draws us away from the very truth it seeks to
uphold.

Contrast this with understanding.

None of us are born understanding. It’s in our nature but requires a conscious effort and
committment that does not come naturally. To understand is to actively put aside our preconceptions,
beliefs, and truths, so that space can be made for new ideas and perspectives. Where judgement seeks
consistency and a complete picture of everything, understanding permits subjectivity and gives rise
to the beauty of multiple perspectives. A draw to deep understanding is the essence of the curious
spirit which sees walls of unrelenting knowledge, and asks, what is beyond here? What else don’t we
know yet? What might we learn? Might something beyond our knowledge one day crumble to reveal
greater truths we have yet to even consider? We aren’t born understanding, but we can learn to
pursue it.

Understanding and judgement are not enemies though. Just as the walls which imprison us hold back
the elements, judgement shields us from obvious lies as much as it keep us from exploring the green
fields of free thought. To judge is to seek safety and refuge in the known, and the understand is to
risk losing that which we hold to be dear.

This is looking much better. Let’s keep going with this instead of the diatribe about the critical
protector. It could be improved though. It’s a little lofty and abstract. Meditate on it and see
what happens.

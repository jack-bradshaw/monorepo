On Nonresistance Other people's words used to sting deeply no matter how gently they were spoken.
Criticism was painful, feedback was frightening, and confrontation was unbearable. My life was a
constant struggle to get by without stepping on an eggshell and potentially upsetting someone, and
often it was this very process that was causing the conflict I so desperately avoided. In time this
perspective has slipped away and been replaced by a gentle and sublime acceptance of the world
exactly as it is without judgment or resistance. From this place of tranquility, a state of near
limitless equanimity, other people have become nothing to fear and their words can no longer hurt
me.

I speak of non-resistance, curiosity, and humility. It is not numbness or dissociation, but a
conscious decision to be authentically present and search for the real meaning in others' words. If
you approach others this way, even the harshest criticism and personal attack becomes little more
than an attempt at communication. You begin to see others as kindred souls, always doing their best
in a hostile universe that doesn’t care for our wellbeing, and potentially unaware of their true
motivations and unconscious desires.

The subtle jab from a friend becomes a plea for connection and a hint at something unresolved. The
sharp rebuke in a meeting becomes an attempt to virtue signal and reveals a fear of rejection and
isolation. The unkind remark from a spouse becomes an opportunity to hear what is really hurting and
deepen the couple's bond. If you can learn to see everything others say and do as an attempt to
communicate their truth, no matter how hurtfully or poorly it is communicated, connection becomes
trivial and peace becomes abundant.

An example comes from my time in group therapy, which to me was an opportunity to practice my
interpersonal skills and refine my social interactions. When another snapped at me one, expressing
sharp disapproval for a question I put to the group, my reaction was embarrassment and fear. What
would happen next, I wondered, would the group chastise me and vote me off the island, so to speak?
What happened next changed my perspective. Although the focus of the group turned to me, the
therapist instead brought their attention to the member who had snapped. With gentle curiosity, he
spent a few moments understanding their motivations and listening without judgement to their
experience. As a group we learned of a time in their distant past when a similar question was asked
by them, and of the sharp rebuke they had received from their authority figures. It turns out they
were not acting with malice towards me, but with fear for our shared wellbeing. They perceived
danger, and reacted as many would.

There is a deeper layer here that we will mention but not examine in too much detail for it deserves
its own essay. In this circumstance, history was trying to repeat itself. Where once they were the
receiver of disapproval, they had now become the dispenser and my role was to be as they once were.
My awareness allowed me to choose a different path, but it’s not difficult to see how this pattern
could repeat across time. If frightened deeply enough, perhaps one day I would become the snapper
and pass this on to a new snappee. We will examine this phenomenon more later, for now let us return
to the topic of non resistance and equanimity.

As time has gone on my perspective has slowly but steadily shifted to incorporate this way of being,
and it has changed how I see not only others but also myself. Where once my own judgements were
either suppressed or expressed, now lives curiosity and a desire to understand what leads me to the
thoughts on my mind and the feelings in my heart. It’s a practice of wanting to know what need is
not being met and what desire is active, and it involves putting aside the surface layer of
conscious experience. When we approach others and ourselves with the knowledge that we are extremely
complex beings with near limitless depth and breadth of soul, curiosity becomes trivial and the
truth becomes apparent. Relationships flourish and love blossoms.

My journey is far from complete and this practice is a joy to refine. As time goes on though, the
muscle becomes stronger and what was once a purely cognitive process is becoming an automatic
unconscious one. I much prefer this approach, for the simple reason there is no longer any need to
be mad at others. Non resistance is the path to inner peace even in the face of overwhelming stress
in others.

I wish there were a way for me to simply share this perspective with others, but in reality it has
been a multi-year process involving therapy three times a week. An intensive regiment, you might
say, of mental exercise and strength training. Here are a few snippets that might help though, and
please take them as a guide more than an absolute set of rules.

- Whenever another person expresses an emotion, no matter how they express it, they are asking to be
  heard. If you give them what they want, you might be surprised how quickly the emotion dissipates,
  which stands in stark contrast to how entrenched it will become when you ignore or dismiss them.
- When another person is talking about you, try to hear what they are saying about themselves.
  Rarely do people mean what they say, especially when emotionally deregulated, and the true meaning
  is often hidden. If you can find that truth, your communication will flourish.
- Hearing others and making space for them does not mean becoming submissive or giving up your own
  needs. Gently and assertively setting boundaries is an art, and it is essential for healthy
  relationships. When others are having difficulty expressing themselves, asking for your needs is
  sometimes a step towards helping them find theirs.

I want to avoid giving too much advice because this is meant to be about a perspective. You already
have within you all the knowledge and experience you need to relate to others, all you need to do
now is learn to detach from your own reactions. Let them come, let them be, and let them go. Choose
to be aware of your reactions, and choose to separate them from your responses. When you begin your
reactions may feel like hailstones, but as you practice they will become a gentle rain.

I cannot shake the feeling this post is not doing equanimity justice. It brings with it a deep calm
that cannot be put into words, and is something we all yearn for in some way. If you join me here,
you will know what I mean. I must reflect now, but hopefully this was helpful.

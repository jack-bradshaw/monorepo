Moving On From Ideas

Writing my recent piece to both of the Phoenix was frustrating for two reasons. I am new to writing
so I was constantly writing reading reconsidering and rewriting the work to change the voice and
tone. More importantly the message I wanted to convey kept evolving and shaping over time. It’s a
long piece and it had various different endings. It was originally about half as long and along the
way I found so many little exits, in some ways this has been a problem in my engineering career as
well. How do you write a piece which examines many possibilities and discard many of them without
writing something absurdly long or unnecessarily meandering? I guess I’m basically asking how do you
write an essay that covers a topic that can guide so many directions but only choose one given that
readers will probably go in their own directions and eventually disagree.

There are a few options. One I’ve seen other authors do is address the arguments as they go,
addressing the alternative avenue, and that’s one approach. It requires anticipating everything your
readers might say though and has the disadvantage of making the text quite long for every paragraph
of something you have to say you might end up with five defending it. I’m not particularly inclined
to give people’s neurotic criticism space and would rather focus on something meaningful the second
alternative is to write a piece and then write a defence to it that strongly argue for it and hope
that anyone who has alternative views. Will in time either prove it wrong or discover that it was
right on their own. Basically I’m saying we could write a very convincing piece that stands on its
own the third option which is the one I’ve chosen and is by far the hardest is to write text in a
way that conveys the meaning create a compelling case and dismantle criticism inherently which is
closer to art and science so it all comes down to writing a piece of text which is meaningful,
effective, and inherently convincing. A huge part of that is who the audience is, because no piece
of text will ever appeal to everybody, which is a fantastic question. Who is my audience? I suppose
my audience is people who like to ask questions people who enjoy going on a mental journey, but I’m
more interested in the journey itself than final answer. People who recognize the world is actually
relatively straightforward and people are willing to accept ambiguity and uncertainty as the true
final answer in most cases I’m not writing for academics who need everything to be put into tiny
little boxes and I’m not writing for spiritual people who want everything to be wishy-washy and feel
good. I’m not writing for political people who need everything to be polarized and electrified and
versus them. I’m not writing for anyone who has a specific idea that they absolutely need validated.
I’m just looking around to see what there is and I’m sharing it with other people in case they like
it so my target audience is people who are just interested in exploring interesting ideas.

What to do what to do. I am fond of my essay and the effort that went into it. I will post it so
others can view it if they want, but with a proviso explaining it was an exercise in essay writing.

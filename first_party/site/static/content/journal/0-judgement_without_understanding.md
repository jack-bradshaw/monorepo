# Judgement Without Understanding

Many years ago at Google a document was written around the time when George Floyd was killed that
declared various terms to be inappropriate for use within the codebase and the corporation at large,
including blacklist/whitelist, master/slave, and a few others. A broad effort was made to remove
these terms from various systems and replace them with alternatives such as allowlist/denylist and
coordinator/director/etc. It was years ago, but I occasionally reflect on this experience, because
something never felt right, and I have finally been able to place it.

In my conversations with the advocates of the effort, and in my reading of their materials, a few
details stand out:

1. Subjective position represented as fact. A canonical example is the interpretation of the word
   “blacklist” to be associated with race. I can understand how a mind overattuned to the lens of
   race might reach that conclusion, but it’s far from an objective connection, and the association
   reflects the beholder, not reality. I found others unwilling to acknowledge this.
1. Cultural positions represented as universal experience. It was almost universally my US-based
   colleagues who cared about these changes (most of them white), and while they demonstrated strong
   concern for US-based social issues, they had virtually no awareness of social issues elsewhere. I
   thought this was ironic given their zeal for inclusion.
1. Unwillingness to consider other perspectives. All conversations on the topic, even those which
   began with genuine curiosity and willingness to be wrong, were treated as attacks to be condemned
   and countered. Without room for curiosity and understanding, it was impossible for anyone to
   truly learn anything novel, and it became difficult to collaborate on meaningful change.
1. Lack of context. I acknowledge that "master/slave" has global historical implications; however,
   in the context of software, it's a sensible abstraction for the exact relationship between two
   machines sometimes. Furthermore, in some context, the term is used respectfully (i.e. BDSM). I
   think it’s clear which context is most meaningful in software engineering, and that context is
   critical to establishing intent and meaning, but that was not acknowledged.
1. Lack of impact. Despite all the fervour around this, I have yet to see evidence connecting their
   efforts with meaningful change, either within the software engineering community, or the broader
   world.

While driven by a desire for racial diversity, the advocates were blind to the diversity of culture
they were destroying, to the necessity of curiosity, and the importance of self-awareness in
subjectivity. A particularly egregious point was a meeting where another engineer suggested that
software engineering be considered a unique sub-group, with its own culture, a position I agree
with, but the advocates argued that software engineering is not a protected status, and thus
software engineering must change to suit the needs of racial equality.

All these points have sat with me for some time, but a deeper point of contention eventually bubbled
to the surface. I saw good people with kind hearts and curious minds be cast down as racist bigots
for the sin of asking sensible questions, challenging a flawed narrative, and suggesting more
sensible alternatives. I saw intelligence be knocked down by dogma, and I saw fear spreading through
a curious society. What it did to some of the finest thinkers I've ever met must be remembered: It
took away their willingness to ask questions.

I know the advocates believe their legacy was making the world a better place, and in some ways
perhaps they did, but the cost of their approach was paid by others who didn’t deserve it. Their own
perspective was held up as fact, to the degree others were expected to comply, and that has
consequences. Ultimately, I cannot respect such people, and am glad we no longer work together. I
can't stand to be around zealots any longer, and do not want to be anywhere near the inevitable and
equally untenable counter movement.

Ultimately, none of this is about racial equality, or software engineering, or even this century. We
all have the power to be a curious mind that asks questions, or a zealot who shouts the same answer
repeatedly, and in our lives we spend time as both. It would be a mistake to assume that judgement
is the cause, and that we should live with pure understanding, but both are necessary for survival.
Instead of condemning judgement, I ask you, what does it take to keep judgement and understanding in
harmony?

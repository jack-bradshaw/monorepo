Introduction:

Before we begin it’s important to take a moment to separate the content from the process. I
understand you may have different views to me, in fact it’s statistically assured we won’t agree on
everything, but let’s step away from that for a moment. I respect that we see things differently, I
have space in my mind for other people’s ideas, and we don’t need to be agreed to move forward here.
In this essay we’re going to focus on something else, we’re going to look at the process of
disagreement. We’re going to talk about what happens when people don’t hold the same views, and
we’re going to focus specifically on what happens when people hold views so radically misaligned
that cancellation becomes a viable and desirable option. We’re going to look at cancel culture
through the lens of conflict.

I understand some will likely ignore my message entirely and write it off as yet another right
winger getting upset about the consequences of their actions. Maybe they will cast me aside as a
misogynist, a xenophobe, a transphobe, or some other label aligned with persona non grata. None of
these would be accurate descriptions, and my political beliefs aren’t aligned with the right, but
that’s beside the point. It doesn’t matter what I believe or what I say, this is about conflict, and
to the untrained mind conflict is an opportunity to destroy.

When we’re talking about cancel culture we’re talking about a highly complex system that crosses the
boundaries between individuals, groups, psychology, neurology, sociology, fiction, fact, and various
other aspects of human existence. It volatile, dangerous, and highly adapted to survive at the cost
of innocent lives. We could look at it as a biohazard even and draw from biology, but let’s not add
needless complexity. Here today, we’ll will focus on its basic properties, with emphasis on what’s
happening for the victim, what going on in the mind of the perpetrator, and how exactly it works.

Defining cancel culture:

It’s the very nature of what we’re discussing to push aside opposing viewpoints and discard nuance,
so it’s natural for those afflicted by the contagion to misinterpret and misunderstand my words. I
acknowledge this reality and have only compassion for their plight. From open minds and open hearts
flow all the treasures of the world, but to a closed mind there is nothing and no one of value. I
feel pity and compassion for people with closed minds, but there is really nothing that can be done
from here. If you aren’t open to considering new ideas in the first place, an essay advocating for
humility and open mindedness is unlikely to be well received.

Cancel culture is a modern term for an ancient practice which I like to call retribution culture.
It’s not new, and as long as there have been humans on this planet it has existed. It hides in every
human heart and may never vanish entirely, but we aren’t merciless victims to it and we have the
ability to resist its destructive power. In this article we will dissect it, study it, and search
for a way to eradicate it as we would with any biohazard.

Let’s first understand exactly what cancel culture is in its modern form. I would describe it as:

A series of behaviours spread culturally whereby ideas and perspectives are regulated using
stigmatisation, shaming, personal attacks, and other forms of socially destructive actions, to the
extent that individuals self-censor and censor others to avoid real or perceived retribution.

Cancel culture as violence:

In essence, cancel culture is about destroying people. It has no interest in truth, humility,
wellbeing, conscientiousness, progress or any other idea worthwhile pursuit. It is nothing more than
a cathartic release of hate, an attempt to punish those deemed worthy of retribution, and it is
inherently destructive. It is fundamentally based in the idea that when we have conflict, the
objective is to destroy the opponent, with no regard to ethics or decency.

Let’s sit what that for a moment. Destroying people. Physically, emotionally, and financially. It’s
chilling snd it makes my stomach turn.

What makes it a form of violence? To answer that we simply need to consider what happens to a person
when they are cancelled? Immediately a divergence presents itself. People respond differently to
cancelation.

We have the rich who can survive it, the poor who will surely perish, and the in between who will be
severely disrupted, potentially for life.

Cancellation is fundamentally a form of culturally approved violence. It hurts people, it damages
relationships, and it breaks society. It may have some advantages and it has certainly been used as
tool to achieve change, but my message for you today is simple. Cancellation is a knife and it can
be a weapon or a tool, but if you’re using it to stab people without concern for their wellbeing,
it’s doesn’t matter to me how righteous you believe you are, it’s violence. No matter how justified
we believe we are, violence only begets further violence. History has shown this time and time
again. We must stop hurting each other, and that begins with turning criticism into curiosity. We
must move from cancellation to humility and look at people with different views not through the lens
of malice but through the lens of opportunity. We must find ways to forgive, learn and grow, not
destroy.

We are all free in our own minds for form a lens through which we see the world. Some might call
this perspective, others will call it world view, but whatever you call it, we all possess the
amazing ability to take the objective and turn it into something subjective. It doesn’t change the
material world and we are all bound by objective reality in some ways, but our ability to make
meaning from meaningless is a beautiful part of being conscious. It’s inherent to us, and it lives
within our own mind. When others tell us what we must think, what we must believe, and what we must
hold sacred though, they are asserting their truth over our own. It’s a form of violence because it
crosses the boundary between individuals and distorts the personal lens. To use threat of violence
to tell another how they must perceive their world is akin to dictating how art shall be viewed, and
it is a form of violence so obscene and abhorrent it should really be a crime. We are talking about
socially enforced brain washing.

One could ask, why not simply maintain your own lens and go about your business? How can other
people possible affect your mind this way? We all retain choice within our own minds, no one can
take away our autonomy if we don’t let them. I hold this view personally, and others don’t have
power over my perspective, but it’s besides the point. Cancellation is an attempt to create
consequences in the objective world for subjective views, and it is therefore an attempt at coercive
control over the individuals inner world. The key detail here is attempt. Murder is still a crime if
you don’t succeed, and rape is still rape with a condom. To even attempt to impose restrictions on
another because of their willingness to hold a different view from you, is violence.

Very well, you may say, you may hold your view but you may not share it. Your view can live within
you, and that’s it. You don’t get to discuss it though and you don’t get to share it. We don’t want
to hear it.

There is some validity in this idea. Just as we should not be forced to take on other people’s
views, we cannot be forced into hearing them. We each maintain the ability to ignore view points we
don’t want, and that’s extends to ignoring speakers. Is there an issue here though? On the surface
no, but with nuance yes.

Putting aside the obvious violence of taking away someone’s livelihood, there’s also a far more
subtle and insidious impact. When we cancel people who have views we disagree with, the message is
clear. If you voice your opinions, you may face the same consequences. I risk those consequences
with this very essay, but that’s besides the points, my life is already forfeit and there is little
time left for retribution. I digress though. When we cancel, we impose our view on others. We take a
position, hold it as truth, and create consequences for anyone who dares disagree. It’s a not a
matter of free speech versus respect as so many would put it, we talking about autonomy within our
own minds. A mind in fear of cancellation is a mind imprisoned, and cancel culture robs us of the
most precious gift: our ability to simply consider new perspectives. Cancellation is a direct threat
to free thought and internal autonomy.

How does this work though? How do other peoples views influence our own minds? Is the mind not its
own universe, and aren’t we free to think anything. In some ways yes, and there will always be those
who refuse to allow others to distort their cognition, but for the most part we are a deeply social
species and those bonds run far below conscious thought. We are talking of course about thought
crime, as Orwell put it, and we need to understand how outward culture affects inwards experience.
How does cancellation warp our thoughts and why is this violence?

Our answer comes down to learning and how it works. We are social creates and learning is a socially
adapted system. We don’t acquire knowledge and wisdom because it’s fun, it’s fun because we were
born to do it. We’re adapted to discover and understand because it helped us survive. We’re also
adapted to share with each other, and learning is a social experience. You don’t have to believe me,
the science is clear (refs). We grow together or we don’t grow at all. No ifs or buts about. We are
stuck together and that’s all there is to it. Our minds are just along for the ride.

This has some rather obvious implications. If we’re learning together but we’re afraid of even
speaking particular ideas, considering strange or disturbing perspectives, and getting distasteful
words out into the open, those thoughts will never be challenged and discarded. Our inherent racism
will simmer and bubble, or views of gender and sexuality will remain as archaic, and our general
hatred for each other will never be examined closely. If we can’t work together, if we can’t admit
our humanity to one another, then our worst tendencies will not get better. They will get worse, and
there is a terrible cost to be paid when that happens.

Free thought, boundless imagination, and unbreakable willpower are the tools which build worlds.
They are found in every author, every philosopher, every scientist, every engineer, thinker, and
lover. Anywhere people are asking questions they are present, and they are the source of all
curiosity. Every one who ever looked at what is and wondered what could be has tasted them, and
those who have mastered them feel their source deep within them. They are the product of curiosity
and humility, and they are our most treasured gifts.

To cancel is to deny the opportunity to learn snd grow, which in turn uproots the vital soil where
our minds flourish. For the enlightened mind cancellation is death by starvation.

So what is a mind to do with ideas that can’t be spoken or acknowledged. Either they go away on
their own accord, or they are locked away. Where do they go though? They run into the shadows where
they cannot be seen, and that is where we must look to find humility. We find humility not by
listening to others, but by listening to the parts of ourselves that can’t won’t be heard. We make
space for our shadow selves, and in doing so free up the mental capacity to make room for others. If
you need proof, simply search the edges of your mind for something you’re not acknowledging, let it
come to the surface, and once you’ve heard it let it go. Like clearing a table of clutter, letting
our unacceptable thoughts come forth is the first step to making space for others.

I wonder how many minds currently subsist on the terror of cancellation. How long have you avoided
ideas, pushed them away, and suppressed their very existence? Tell me how it feels to lock your
thoughts up for fear of retribution. Are you happy? I doubt it. So much for making the world a
better place, the apparent goal of the canceler.

The perpetrators:

When the ancient eastern philosopher Lau Tsu spoke of agents of death, those of rigid minds and
hearts, he was speaking of people who cannot bend with the changing moment. Such people meet new
information with resistance, they seek confirmation of their existing understanding, and they push
against the flow. In essence they force their will on the world and are shocked when the world
pushes back. It’s a simple and effective analogy for anyone who sees conflicting ideas as an
opportunity to destroy another instead of collaborating and growing, particularly given the very
literal death they create.

Why do we cancel?

To build a better path forward we need to understand why people cancel one another. In a nutshell:
Fear of shame, which is really just fear of becoming an outcast. We desperately need to be part of
our social group, any social group, and many will bow at the pressure of the collective. We fear
being left out in the cold, and so when culture dictates adherence to dogma we either drink the cool
aid or go off on our own. I present you with an alternative: Humility, curiosity, and courage.

Humility is a virtue that unlocks all other virtues. It allows us to look at ourselves and others
gently, and ask, what could we be missing? It gives us the power to consider new perspectives, find
our deepest errors, and forge a new path. With humility we look at the world and ask, what are you
trying to tell me?

In contrast to humility, fear and shame keep us in the dark. A mind bound with fear and a heart
poisoned with shame cannot see beyond their own perception, and so the subjective world becomes the
objective. In fear we no longer experience the possibility of danger, we see material harm. In shame
we move past relativity and opinion and enter the realm of worthy and unworthy individuals. When the
untrained mind blurs the line between perception and reality, the effect can be rather toxic to
anyone in their immediate viscosity, which unfortunately, in a world with the information
technology, includes practically everyone.

What are we to do? It’s quite simple really, we must begin by acknowledging the fundamental
limitation of humanity: We can always change ourselves, and there is very little we can do to change
others. This limitation may be frustrating, but only if we need to change others. What if we stop
playing by the rules of cancel culture for a minute, and just let it be? What if we view the
cancellers as nothing more than online trolls and stop feeding them?

When trolls are left on their own they fizzle out. It’s just in their nature. They want attention
and engagement, so when they are denied it they have nothing to subsist on. I wonder though, can we
accelerate the process? I believe we can. Trolls want more than attention, they want you to become
triggered. They need you to become emotionally activated to fuel some pathological desire. If we
engage but remain curious and courageous, we become poison to them.

When I encounter such people, I let them speak. I understand them, and then I look for the trauma
behind the mask. I ask questions to get to the root cause of whatever is bothering them. It’s a form
of passive opposition because it plays their game without satisfying their expectations. They desire
argumentation and conflict, but they can’t survive in the fire of genuine curiosity and compassion.
At each turn their psychological defences will attempt to pull you into their illusion but it’s a
choice. Filter out their controls, ignore their coercion, and stand firmly within your own mind. Let
the fire of understanding light the path forward and watch as their inner demons fall by the
wayside. We are all enough, even the cancellers, and we only need to expose their lies to the light.

Conclusion

At its core, cancel culture is based on cruelty and hubris. It arises from seeing others struggling
with their inner demons and inherent human flaws, finding ourselves better, and pointing the finger
of shame. It subsists on the smug joy that comes from holding socially acceptable views, of being
part of the in-crowd, and feeling superior to others. It elevates the canceler to a perverse
position of power and authority at the cost of other people’s wellbeing and the overall wellbeing of
society. It is heralded by its proponents as a means to make the world a better place, to write the
wrongs of colonialism, racism, and whichever other ism we have decided to hate today, but it
fundamentally betrays its own cause for one simple reason: It is a form of violence, and we are
never justified when we use violence to achieve our goals. I’m sure the proponents will disagree,
but everyone who uses violence believes they are justified.

I pose a simple thought experiment. Step out of time for a moment and consider all of human history
as a novel on a shelf. Flick through the pages and notice the patterns from antiquity to now. Do you
notice something about conflict? Look carefully at the victors and find even one who thought they
weren’t justified in their actions. If you can, see if there is a single group who used violence
without causing irreparable harm to innocent lives. You can’t, because it’s impossible. Violence by
definition is wilful harm. Every act of violence creates a tear in the fabric of humanity, and no
amount of cutting will ever sew up the gaping holes we have created across the ages. It is self
destructive and it must stop now

Stepping back into the present, we have a choice before us.

On one hand you can put down your weapons, let go of spite, soften your convictions, and make space
in your heart for people you disagree with. Instead of arguing and digging your feet in, you can
yield and let other people be wrong. You might be surprised how quickly other people let go of their
terrible ideas if you engage them with genuine curiosity and a willingness to collaborate, and in
time you too may even discover the terrible ideas you hold.

On the other hand we can keep cancelling each other. We can continue to use coercive control to
manipulate one another, we can continue poisoning our minds with fear and shame, and we can let the
tools that built our world be lost to the ages. Go ahead and force your views on other people, smile
with smug satisfaction as you destroy nuance and decorum, and take pleasure as you show the world
you’re one of the good ones. I can’t imagine it will end poorly for anyone.

It’s your choice, and until we learn to make space for others to be wrong we aren’t going anywhere.
You’re free to do whatever you like, but count me out. I have no interest in being part of the
malicious violence we in the 21st century call cancel culture. It’s just retribution disguised as
virtue, and I have precious time for that bullshit.

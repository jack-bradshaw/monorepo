The Dangling Thread Every argument and school of thought has a weakness. A simple thread which can
be pulled to unravel the tapestry completely. It’s not a flaw it’s actually a matter of design. You
see, we are imperfect beings and our attempts to create perfection inevitably fail. How could a mind
shaped by millennia of surviving near death experiences possibly hope to create something divine.
All we are good at is living long enough to tick anther day off the calendar. Where it all leads is
the contemplation of our complete and absolute inability to be certain about anything. Not in an
extreme or absolute sense, but to the degree that no system or thought can explain everything
forever.

We see this everywhere, and we can find a particularly potent example in science. It’s a tale woven
across history which began well before Copernicus and stretches back to even before antiquity. As
long as there has been a mind asking how does it work, there has been a scientist. Our ancestors
didn’t have the tools we do, but they created systems we can still use today to understand. In time
those systems were subsumed by better one, each a little less inaccurate and a little more detailed.
Fast forward Millenia and we have the Bohr model of the atom, the germ theory of disease, and the
statistical model of thermodynamics. Only a few years later we moved on to greener pastures and took
only the best parts of our theories with us. At each stage the next step was taken by looking at a
beautiful idea and asking, what’s not quite right here? What is missing, and what happens if we tug
on that thread?

In time the Bohr model was shown to be only partially accurate and a more complex model took its
place. We stopped considering electrons to be particles moving like planets around a sun, and
learned to view them as probability distributions, with some chance of being in many different
places. Even this model has limitations, and we eventually learned that electrons are not absolutely
indivisible, they are composed of quarks. All this to say, keep looking and you’ll eventually find a
dangling thread.

I wonder where else we can look and what other threads we can find. What happens if we stop pulling
on the threads of the theory and start pulling on the threads of the system itself? What becomes of
science’s we try to find a flaw in its very foundational logic. Could it be possible to undo physics
the way Gödel showed us the error of trying to find a perfectly completely system of mathematics?
They are not too far apart, and it’s certainly tempting.

Or perhaps instead of trying to pull the rug out from underneath physics, perhaps we can take a step
further and try to find a hole in reason itself. Not the mathematical systems we use to express it
or the systems of science we build atop it, but the very foundational logic upon which they rest.
Can we dissolve reason?

How would we do this if not with reason? To use reason to dissolve itself would be circular, but in
many ways we already use reason to define itself. Our axioms are assumed because they make
reasonable sense, for example how would 1 + 1 possibly be anything other than two, it’s obvious, but
only if we assume truth has basis in reason. What happens if we suspend reason in search of a more
solid foundation?

I have an answer but it’s a little difficult to put into words. It most closely resembles a joke
perhaps. It raises its hand only when I let myself consider a world unbound by rules, and it ducks
its head when rules take their hold. Childish and giddy, it holds the answer.

Moving beyond reason opens the door to humanity. We aren’t reasonable people, if history is to be
believed, and reason rarely lasts very long when it takes hold. It’s not to say reason doesn’t hold
sway over what happens, and certainly the universe seeks to obey some very rigid rules, but rather
to say it’s not the foundation for humanity. We stand upon emotion and experience, and in them we
find reality.

Perhaps though we’re following the wrong thread if we head in that direction. We’re not looking for
an explanation of human experience, we’re considering the boundaries of systems. How we interpret
and perceive them is both irrelevant and misleading. We could always assume our personal experience
is all that exists, and that’s a topic for another day, but for now let’s proceed under the
assumption some reality exists around us.

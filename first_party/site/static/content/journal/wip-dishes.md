Dishes Every morning my dishes need to be done. I have a dishwasher, and it helps with the efforts,
but it doesn’t eliminate the ever present task. I will be loading and unloading it every day for the
rest of my life until someone finds a way to automate the task. I don’t like waiting for others to
solve my problems and set out to do it myself.

The obvious solution is disposable equipment, but there are downsides that make this untenable. It’s
a highly wasteful solution and is hard to apply to the more robust cooking utensils. A sustainable
solution is required.

The next step involves adding automation into the mix. Washing is already automated by the
dishwasher, it’s just the loading and unloading that needs to be automated, plus perhaps the
pre-washing stage for burned-on food. A simple robot arm could work, but for greater reach and
dexterity a humanoid robot might be better. I find myself averse to this solution though. Whenever
we replace humans with humanoid robots it’s a sign we’re automating the task, not the outcome.
Humans are machines that evolved to hunt, eat, reproduce, and perform a few other tasks, we aren’t
necessarily the best design for automation.

Moving away from humanoid robots means rethinking the solution overall. We can’t use disposable
stuff, but perhaps there’s another way to eliminate the problem entirely. Why do we need to eat? For
energy obviously, that’s really what food is for, so can we find a way to get energy that doesn’t
require all the effort and waste of cooking and cleaning? Can we entirely eliminate the kitchen?

We could maybe create photosynthetic humans with bioengineering, but even plants require nutrients
through their roots so that wouldn’t actually solve the full issue.

Food is also difficult because it requires knowledge of nutrition, time to eat, brushing teeth, etc.
let’s think radically.

We could get all our nutrients with an intravenous solution. Our digestive systems would atrophy but
that’s great, especially for bottoms. Unfortunately it’s expensive. If we could use economies of
scale though we might be able to produce IV fluid for cheap. Then we just need a hot-swappable arm
mounted IV solution holder to deliver it to the person's bloodstream.

Imagine that. Snap in a new IV pack. You’re good to go. No eating, drinking, pooping, or cleaning.
No acid reflux, IBS, or other digestive issues (except atrophy, maybe we can manage that with
pharmaceutical solutions). Just snap in a pack and go.

Somehow I don’t see this breaking through. I’m sure there’s a niche of people who would happily take
the trade off, but human beings overall like their natural ways of living. Food and eating are just
so deeply ingrained in people and culture, accepting this idea would be closer to accepting an iron
lung for the rest of your life to simplify indoor air quality. It can’t be invasive, it can’t
radically disrupt the foundation of human culture, and it can’t require trading off efficiency for
joy. I think people want to eat.

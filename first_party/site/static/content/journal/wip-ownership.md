Ownership What is ownership at its core? If I say to you that I own shares in a company, what am I
actually saying? If I tell you that I own my house, what does that mean? If I tell you that I own my
clothes, could you take them from me? If I tell you that I own another person, what is actually
involved in slavery? Let's take a deep dive into what ownership actually means fundamentally, given
that it's something we all as humans seem to have some understanding of. What is ownership? That is
the question we seek to answer.

Let's take some common examples, things that everyone in the Western and Eastern world are familiar
with. Many people own their house. Many people do not. What's the difference between these two
people? You might immediately say, I have some intrinsic sense that when I own my house it is
completely mine; I can do what I want with it. Access to myself is free. There is no cost to its
use. In other words, what we’re trying to say is ownership of something is a stable position where
no more effort must go in to change ownership. A renter does not own their house, both on paper or
in spirit, because they must continue paying money over time for access to that property. In
contrast, a homeowner does not need to keep paying money for access to that property. Or do they?

You see, owning a home is not without cost. Putting aside maintenance, which is simply the cost of
paying down entropy and disorder, in most places there is also a property tax. The owner of the
house must pay fees to the local, federal, and state governments of wherever they live by virtue of
being the landowner. Unlike other asset taxes, property tax is incurred while you hold the asset,
not at the time of disposition, such as when selling stocks and making a capital gain. In this way,
a homeowner could be seen not only to be owning their home but to be owning an obligation to their
local government. Given that these two things cannot be acquired separately, and owning a house
always means owning an obligation to pay someone else money, we could ask the question: Do
homeowners actually own their homes? Does owning something truly count as ownership if there are
unavoidable fees attached, by which we don't mean fees you can choose not to pay? For example, I can
choose to allow the home that I own to fall into disarray if I stop paying the maintenance cost, but
I can't choose not to pay the property taxes.

In that respect, is ownership of a home not more akin to renting the land from the government? Are
homeowners not simply renters who have been tricked into believing that they are owners? It’s
monetarily a better position, generally speaking, as you hold the title for an asset that
appreciates in value. But perhaps we need to examine why it appreciates. In my time on this planet,
I have observed amongst many people an almost unanimous view that owning a property is a good
long-term investment, and there is obvious financial data to support this. But as we know, financial
systems are not objective; they are deeply bound to human subjectivity and are ultimately a
representation of what we value. We value owning homes. Therefore, homes are valuable monetarily. So
the question is not, is owning a home a financially worthwhile investment? It’s, why do human beings
believe that owning a home is a good idea, and would they still believe this if the obviousness of
financial taxes—I mean property taxes—undermining the very concept of ownership were made more
apparent? What I'm trying to say is, would people still care about owning a home if they started to
see it as renting from the government versus actually owning something in the sense that they have
come to know ownership: that it is theirs without cost, or should I say, without further cost,
because that’s really the difference between ownership and renting—no further cost to acquire or
hold?

I think therein lies the definition of ownership. Ownership occurs when there is no further cost to
simply hold the asset, when holding the asset is the stable, low-energy state. So I suppose, in a
way, it actually makes sense that you cannot own land in the truest sense. Across human history,
wars have been fought repeatedly simply to acquire more land from other people, land being a primary
dependency of virtually all human activities. So there is a constant and unavoidable threat of
possible acquisition from other people. And in a sense, either through insurance, referral to
another party through taxes, or personal willingness to use violence, owning and holding land is
inherently bound to defending that land from violent acquisition by others. And it is in violent
acquisition by others that we find the fundamental reason you cannot own land. There is always the
cost of defense. Contrast this with—wait, never mind.

Contrast this with the price of something intangible, such as stocks, bonds, or some other form of
ownership where the asset itself represents little more than an abstract contract between two
parties. The more abstract, the more intangible the asset, the easier it is to defend from physical
acquisition. For example, ownership of a company can be manifested by a contract which simply says a
party owns some portion of the company. So long as that contract is kept safe, either by being
duplicated or stored in a safe place, then that ownership is safe unless the very foundations of the
system crumble, such as a government being overthrown and a new regime invalidating existing
contracts. Then it is far easier to defend that ownership. The critical differences are: the asset
itself is intangible; the contract itself is between private individuals. No, actually, I’m not sure
that’s true. That’s probably the same for house ownership. It doesn't matter who the contract is
between. What matters is that its defense cost is considerably lower. I can physically move onto
someone’s land and deny them access by being there and having force. It is much harder to physically
move into a company and deny access with force, one because companies are generally much larger and
beyond the scope of one individual, but more because companies and other assets represent intangible
constructs. They represent groups of people; they represent intellectual property, processes,
products, consumers, brands. They represent things which cannot so easily be controlled by having a
gun. They are intellectual in their defense.

So what we have ultimately is a house as a social contract between both the owner, the government,
and the people, which says you have primary access to this property; however, you must continue
paying money for that access, and therefore it is not the true form of ownership. It is simply a
form of renting. Defense of an area contrasted with more intangible ownership, such as ownership of
an asset which has a far lower defense cost by virtue of the asset itself being harder to occupy, to
some degrees being virtually impossible to occupy, or occupation not being a meaningful construct.
In that way, ownership of assets is less of a social contract and more of an abstract—I hesitate to
say reality. Is it still a social contract? It’s just far more abstracted.

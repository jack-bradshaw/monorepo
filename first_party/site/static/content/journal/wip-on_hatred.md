Hatred cannot be contained, because it communicates an important message like all emotions. It
screams for change and pushes for better conditions, and every moment of suppression is a moment of
reinforcement. In time suppressed hate grows until it can be contained no further, and when it comes
forth it does so with destructive force. If we want to limit the pain caused by hatred we cannot
suppress it, we must instead find healthy ways to let it roam free and leave us in peace. In time
humanity must lean this if we are to survive the challenges of existence in a hostile universe.

I find myself ironically feeling considerable hatred towards the sanctimonious ones in our society.
It's difficult to determine exactly what is so awful about them. It could be the way they praise
each other for their purity, unaware of how virulent and destructive their actions are, or it could
be the clear disdain they show for others, despite their apparent message of peace. No, it's neither
of those. I despise the way they shame and suppress the humanity of others, and in their desire to
create a paradise free of their perceived harms they have unintentionally created a monster. An
inhuman beast that will not be satisfied until everyone is as scared and afraid as they are. I
despise their unwillingness to have courage in the face of their obvious cultural issues, and their
decision to propagate harm instead of challenge and neutralise it. I despise the irresponsibility of
those who espouse puritanical adherence to what they perceive as right and wrong. All they create is
more hurt, all they do is perpetuate cycles of pain, and all they do is make themselves feel smug
and superior. I wish they would simply be quiet.

Two weeks later

Hatred is a deeply human experience worthy of respect and admiration. It’s hatred which protects our
young from predators, and its hatred which keeps us safe when threatened. In hatred we find
humanity's unwillingness to be snuffed out and our deepest desire for solidarity. It goes without
saying though, unrefined and unprocessed hatred are deeply destructive forces, so how can we
reconcile hatred with love and generosity?

It comes down to repression and shame. If we look at someone holding hate as unworthy of love and
make them an outcast, we send them a clear message. You are not enough to be included in our group.
You belong on your own, and you are not welcome here. It’s not hard to see how this would only lead
to further resentment and hatred towards their perceived in-group. If we treat hate as unacceptable
we only create more of it. What do we do instead?

It begins with compassionate curiosity. When hatred is met with genuine compassion for the holder
and curiosity about their unique human experience, we create the conditions for them to explore
themselves, separate what was given to them from what they want to bring forward into their life,
and allow them space to process their repressed experiences. In this space people holding hatred are
seen as human beings grappling with a deeply human experience, not undesirable outcasts too damaged
and broken to be fixed.

Of course, it’s also understandable to hate people who hold hate. It’s obvious how many crimes and
atrocities have been committed by the hate in our hearts, and no one could be blamed for having
grown contemptuous and suspicious of those who hold it. In such hate we find our inherent desire to
prevent the ills of the past from repeating, but ironically no matter how justified our hate feels,
when we act on hate we risk becoming the very thing we hate most. Hatred for hate requires the same
compassionate curiosity so that we can explore and express it safely without repeating the painful
experiences of the past.

What is not compassionate curiosity though? I suppose it comes down to intention and attitude.
Compassion requires holding a genuine interest in the soul of others and a willingness to nurture
their inherent worth. Curiosity requires holding space for what could be and putting aside what
appears to be. When combined, they create a tool for seeing the real human behind the hate,
understanding what that hate does for them, and helping them let it go so they can move past it. If
compassion is alone though it risks becoming judgemental compassion which seeks to mould and change
the other person towards some ideal, and if curiosity is used without compassion it becomes little
more than obtuse questioning and interrogation. In conjunction they heal, but in isolation they
destroy. In absence of both, no healing can ever come to pass.

In summary, hatred is human and nothing human is ever wrong. We are all born with our shared cross
to bear, our nature, and compassionate curiosity about that nature is the key to moving beyond our
current limitations and letting hate be present without controlling us. In contrast to rejection and
suppression which only exacerbate hatred, compassionate curiosity allows for actual growth.

All that being said, compassionate curiosity is not about reinforcing hatred or creating further
prejudice. It would be counterproductive to help others become more hateful or reinforce their
existing prejudice, but this isn’t what happens in compassionate curiosity. In my conversations with
myself and others, it was clear that when hatred is met with non-judgement and its roots are
understood with curiosity, it naturally dissipates on its own. It survives off conflict, and it
withers and dies when gently examined and acknowledged for its original purpose: safety in a complex
world before we had complex thought.

Hatred is always human, hatred for hatred is particularly human, and compassionate curiosity is the
pathway to deep integration of our inherent humanity. If we practice it with ourselves and the
people around us, making space for hate and malice while opening ourselves to their inherent value
and purpose, maybe we might finally achieve the peaceful world we desperately seek.

I found something remarkable today while meditating on another topic. Hatred is the foundation of
all ethics. We aren't rational creatures, and all our logic and reason for our actions is simply the
wrapping we use to justify it and resolve the finer details. At its core, below all the sophistry,
is hatred. It's hatred which binds right and wrong, its hatred which compels us to protect the weak
from the vultures who would exploit them, and it's hatred which drives us towards equity and
equality. If you look deep enough into any ethical belief, strip away all sophistry and politeness,
and just get to the core of what you want, you will find hatred. Deep, pure, hatred. Accept the
hatred with all your heart, give it space within you, and something beautiful will happen. You will
move beyond right and wrong, and learn the truth. All moral reasoning leads back to hate, and it
feels good to hate.

In all my time on this earth my ethical compass has been tested, but something always pulled it in
the right direction even when life was tugging hard in the wrong direction. I have finally come to
understand what that is. It's not love, it's not reason, and it's not wisdom. It's simply pure
unadulterated hate for unnecessary suffering. In this root my compass is firmly established, and
from this root every derivative form of ethics can be found. I hate that which creates unnecessary
suffering, and nothing could be more human.

I know there are some in society who see hatred as unacceptable, who would take my message and call
it hate-think or hate speech, but that could not be further from the truth. Hatred is as human as
skin and bone, and to reject hatred is to reject humanity. I ask you, when confronted with someone
who accepts humanity entirely and someone who would silence and cast out those who dare be human,
who is truly acting on hatred?

Potential insert. It is hatred which empowers mothers to defend their children from predators, it is
hatred which binds bitter enemies together when confronted with an unassailable adversary beyond the
might of any one nation, and it is hatred which pushes death back and wears down disease. Hatred is
so deeply entrenched in humanity for good reason, and it must be protected.

Of course hatred is an emotion but not an end. It doesn't come from nowhere, and it has a cause like
all other systems. I examined hatred in myself and others, and found it to often be based in fear.
How fitting and not at all surprising. If we drill through intelligence, plow through dissonance,
and get to the core of what drives our so called ethical behavior, we find hatred, only to look
deeper and find fear. The most primal and primitive of all emotions. The aversion to danger and the
captor of all curiosity. It fits perfectly. We begin as infants, knowing nothing but fear and
reward, so if we drill down through ethics of course this is what we will find. Ethics is just
abstraction over abstraction, complexity built upon itself, all to find more complex ways of
managing fear. If you want to understand ethics, you have to confront fear, and if you want to
confront fear, you have to put aside ethics. Only once we understand fear can we be free of it, and
only once we are free of it can we build a system of ethics which is truly independent of our past.
If you truly seek ethical existence, then I encourage you to confront your deepest horrors.

Fear is again just a reaction though. I fear being hurt, as do most people. Not superficially, but
deeply. I have known hurt, the deepest and most painful kind, the type that stays with you for
decades and doesn't heal with time. It's that hurt which drives my ethics, for it's the hurt no one
should have to experience. It's the hurt of being treated as an object. All ethical failures stem
from our inability to recognise personhood and act accordingly. It's evident from the smallest scale
human interactions, where one party puts their needs above the others with dissonance and deflection
as their tools of dehumanisation, to the grand scale holocaust we humans subject other species to.
In failure to see the personhood of others, we give ourselves the tools to move against the most
fundamental ethical principle, that people must not unnecessarily suffer.

To wrap this all up, we began with a question about spirituality, were guided by introspection to
consider the depths of hatred, and found hatred to be a deeply human experience often rooted in
fear, and by examining it compassionately instead of rejecting it as shameful and unwelcome, it was
revealed to be the product of fear. When considering fear, the greatest being depersonalisation and
objectification, we discovered the means by which all atrocities are committed. In failure to see
personhood we grant ourselves a license to create unnecessary suffering, and in minimization of that
suffering we find the root of ethics. I have come to believe ethics is not a rigid system of rules,
a search for greater utility or good, but a simple process of seeing reality and recognising
personhood. If we can see others as people, surely we have everything we need to do what is right.

Why though? What makes pain so fundamental? I think the answer is simple. Existence in a hostile
universe is fraught with perils, and hurting is the most foundational experience any living being
can have. It's pain which pushes us away from danger, and every living being needs a sense of pain
to survive. Whether physical or emotional, it's the one universal constant which unites all living
beings.

Paradox of Tolerance Revision I’m writing this essay in response to an uptick in posts on Bluesky
regarding the paradox of tolerance. I observed support and disapproval of the paradox, that society
cannot grant limitless tolerance to intolerant agents, itself being a form of intolerance, and have
decided to form my own view on the topic.

Capture Off the cuff, it seems a little like sweeping the problem under the rug and hoping it will
go away. We can’t just declare intolerance for the intolerant and hope it disappears. It is an
effect, and the cause must be addressed to actually solve the issue. I suppose there’s something to
be said for disrupting the flow of intolerance, but that’s worth investigating further.

There’s an element of gatekeeping too. Who decides what counts as intolerance enough to be silenced,
and who determines whether those people are worthy? If we take a simple statistical bet on people,
the majority of the non-racists and non-sexists in the USA right now eat meat and drink animal milk,
products which produce massive suffering, so it’s highly debatable whether such people are as
morally upstanding as they claim to be.

If we back away from whether it’s right or wrong and view it anthropologically instead of ethically,
is intolerance not a deeply human trait? I see humanity on both sides of the discussion, and who am
I to judge people for simply expressing and following their nature. Hatred and intolerance are
deeply human, so perhaps it’s not an issue of suppressing intolerance, and we can put aside all
paradoxes relating to when it’s appropriate to suppress it and simply address the root causes while
giving people an outlet in the meantime.

Funny how so many quandaries end up here. If we accept humans radically, with all their flaws and
irregularities, then it simply becomes a matter of creating a space to express and neutralize the
darker aspects. If we use intolerance and drive ideas underground, we simply create an avenue for
unconscious suppression and all the demons it unlocks.

I hold it fairly important to distinguish between views and actions because views alone are
powerless without motivated actors. Holding a view has no impact unless we give it agency, and the
power of metacognition and introspection cannot be ignored when considering one’s ability to hold a
view but not act on it. It’s a vitally important factor because views are not always within our
control, particularly given the disappearance of neuroplasticity later in life, and all we can do
past a certain point is gently nudge our views in a better direction and maintain meta-cognitive
control over their influence. In essence, we must tolerate views because views are sometimes beyond
change, within control, and always personal.

Actions are an entirely separate matter because actions invariably commit changes to the world and
often cannot be revoked. A society unable to limit the actions of its members is entirely at the
mercy of its most violent and pathological individuals, and it's fairly obvious that those affected
by actions have a right to act against actions which impact them and are harmful. What counts as
harmful and where the line is drawn is a complex matter as always.

More to say.

First let’s get the bitterness out of the way. I hold disdain for most philosophy because it tends
to overcomplicate simple matters and is vastly inferior to science as a means of understanding
reality. Let’s make space for it though.

It addresses the downstream consequences (intolerance) without addressing the upstream causes
(variable and complex). Treating symptoms is clearly an ineffective answer that does nothing to
address the source of the issues, and it has the unfortunate byproduct of simply creating tension
between our desires for the world and its natural state.

As always, the philosophers have taken a rather simple social interaction and overcomplicated it
with endless philosophical sophistry.

Let’s try that again.

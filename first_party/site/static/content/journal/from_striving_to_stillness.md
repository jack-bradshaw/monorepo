# From Striving to Stillness

Revision 2

It has been seven years since my journey into therapy began, and what once seemed like routine
maintenance has become a fundamental shift within me. Initially, therapy was merely a way to go
about my week, a way to release the stress of my interactions at work, and a tool for maintaining my
relationship with my spouse. However, as the years went on it became the path to something closer to
philosophy and spirituality. In time my destination disappeared, my needs transformed, and the work
became less about maintenance and instead turned to growing a completely new mindset. In essence, my
focus moved from maintaining my life to building a completely new one.

Some of my relationships from my old life survive, for example my spouse and I remain deeply in love
with the aid of the occasional "yes dear", but many of my attachments to the past have disappeared.
In their place exists something new, something very difficult to explain or put into words, but I
will try. It begins with a choice, a decision on how to view the world and everything beyond my
control. Where once lived frustration, now lies equanimity, and in place of resistance to unforeseen
consequences exists the will to accept the world as it is. Judgement has given way to curiosity, and
my compulsive need for order has become openness to experience. At times this new perspective does
bring up worries, for a lack of clarity is in its own way concerning, but by opening myself to
possibilities that do not fit nicely with what is already held to be true, my understanding has
grown.

In essence my mind has shifted to accommodate ideas and perspectives that challenge my understanding
at the deepest layers, and the joy of life that eluded me for so long has come to fruition. A firm
grasp of knowledge is a solid claim to power, but power corrupts and inevitably spells its own
demise. By letting go of certainty and embracing the unknown, my power has been replaced with
something more important: Grace.

I recall one particular time when this new system of being was of particular importance to me. My
spouse was expressing frustration towards me, not because of me, but towards me nonetheless. His
concerns with the world were understandable, and he needed someone to speak with. My old systems of
being would have perceived this frustration as danger, impending malice, and something to be
contained, yet my new systems brought something forward that was previously incomprehensible:
Gratitude. Here was my beloved, expressing his emotions to me, and giving me the trust needed to
share his thoughts. Not only was he sharing openly, he was breaking new ground in his willingness to
challenge ideas and view the world differently. Had my old systems not been so thoroughly
dismantled, this would not have come to pass. I learned much about my spouse that day, and our
connection deepened. This is what happens when fear becomes curiosity, and the need for certainty
becomes the love of ambiguity.

I find myself now in a place where the path here is both obvious but also extremely difficult to
explain. Like a child learning to walk, it was a process of failure and repetition until eventually
success. I stumbled, got hurt, and eventually found a way to walk on my own. I find myself wondering
if perhaps my path contains lessons that might help others on their journey towards
self-actualisation, and how my ideas might be spread. Could there be a way to help others grow?
There must be.

This mindset is not set in stone, and on occasion I find myself slipping back into my old ways. It
is to be expected given the architecture of this mind. I have come to realize my path is non-linear
and winding, but more importantly the core of my being is not indivisible. We humans live in the
illusory world of the singular self and often fail to realise the obvious truth: Within us live many
faces, many minds, and many beings. Often overlapping, but not always coherent and sometimes
misaligned. We move in many directions, bound together by the idea of a self, like a pack of wild
animals choosing the survival of the group over the desires of the individual. In states of deep
meditation this becomes obvious, and the deafening roar of unheard voices breaches the surface.
Always there, always direction, but rarely seen.

This is all to say, progress is not constant because the parts of us move at different speeds.
Lessons grasped by some pieces may take years for others to understand. What is obvious to one, is
unclear to another, perhaps to the degree of being beyond comprehension. Like children playing the
three legged race, true progress requires compassion for ourselves and recognition of the varied
nature of our inner beings.

As these words leave my fingers and enter the page, a subtle shift stirs within me. I feel the parts
of me moving together as one, and my thoughts are taking on a unified stillness. Clearly distinct
from the freeze associated with fear, a state very familiar to me, this is serenity. When all parts
of my ecosystem work together in harmony, there is nothing left to do but be present with them. No
choice to be made, no fight to be won. In moments like these when inner peace reveals itself, all
that is left to do is smile.

Perhaps that is the true shift that has occurred within me over these years. I have learned to
simply be, without striving or doing. By practicing non-judgement, detachment, and equanimity, the
inner turmoil of the past has slipped away like a snake shedding its skin. It was never truly my
skin to begin with though, and was mere dressing given to me by others before I was aware. This
realization does raise the question though, what actually led here? How can I help others find
peace?

The answer is, perhaps, that there is no answer. Nothing can be done for others, because there is
nothing that needs doing. Peace, or the potential for it, exists within all of us, forever and
always. It was there the moment we were born and lingers even after we die. Like ripples on the
surface of a pond, time is all it takes for the stillness to return. Action would only seek to
exacerbate the turmoil. Just give it time, be compassionate to those around you, and eventually
everything will fall into place.

One day humanity will find peace, and when that happens we will look around us and realise, we were
always here. We simply needed to learn how to see.

As I prepare to publish, a voice within me cries out in fear and frustration. Humanity will never
find peace, it claims, we must always fall back into our old ways. War and conflict are a part of
us, and we cannot escape them. No peace lasts, and no journey is ever complete. Nothing of substance
has been offered here, and this text is mere sentiment and poem. What have you to say for yourself?

This is, of course, the striving mind that speaks here, an artifact of my old ways. Always needing
certainty, structure, and the familiarity of the rigid. Suspicious of everything, under the guise of
curiosity, but forever guarded against the formless and the possibility of beauty. It walks a path
with only two outcomes, to become ever more like stone and remain firmly fixed in what is certain,
or be one with the ever changing course of progress and let the rough edges be worn down. In the
end, this choice is an illusion. My heart is open forever now, it's simply a matter of time before
this voice is washed away.

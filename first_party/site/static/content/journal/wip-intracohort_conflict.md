Intro-Cohort Conflict At every stage of human moral development we find people understanding
themselves and learning to make better decisions, and in doing so learning when to cast off their
existing systems and form new ones. As newborns we begin with pain, knowing nothing but what prods
us and what we strive for. As children we begin to ask, what is beyond pain and what can be gained
for myself, and as we discover reward we learn balance. Soon though empathy emerges and with it
comes significant complexity. We discover others, find strength in numbers, and learn to put the
needs of others above ourselves. In simple niceties we appease each other, gaining the benefit of
unity and the pleasure of companionship, only to soon find consequences as the effects of our
actions compile. In pursuit of more sustainable good we splinter, and with contracts make manifest
our agreements, giving rise to individual liberty and collective cohesion. A system which works for
many but is ultimately flawed, for the shifting sands of chance endlessly put some above others and
cast even the most reasonable contracts into disarray. In pursuit of something truly eternal we find
reason, logic, and abstraction. Tools for analysis and understanding, giving rise to systems of
limitless complexity. A point where no further development seems necessary, until the mind itself
becomes the subject of understanding. In observing ourselves, in reduction of the soul to scientific
data, we unlock a question. Why do we pursue the good? Ethics itself becomes the topic of inquiry,
and through the varied fields of science we find answers in the objective world. In psychology we
discover the conscious and unconscious, in neurology we discover a rats nest of complexity, and in
anthropology we discover the very words you read now. In consideration of evolution though we find
the unfortunate but painfully obvious conclusion that ethical behaviour is little more than
adaptation to natural selection, a conclusion which challenges the very purpose of ethics. If we are
nothing more than machines surviving at all costs, slaves to an uncaring universe, should ethics be
discarded? It cannot exist without choice, and in choice we find an opportunity to free ourselves
from our commitment to good, but do we? No. We made the choice long ago to put the needs of others
into consideration, and the decision stands. Others matter. We may not be truly free, but we are
still here, and we become one no longer for personal gain, but for recognition that we are the same.
A transcendence of self and a commitment to others. It may go further than this, but put depth aside
and consider interaction. We are one but we are all growing at different rates, so what happens as
people at different stages interact? In consideration of intra-cohort conflict we find a more
dilemma of recursive difficulty. How do we coexist with other humans who are still learning to be
good, given our mutually contradictory definitions of good? Many have answered this, creating
systems and schools of thought, but perhaps there is a simpler answer devoid of all top-down
administration and decree. I wonder whether everyone already has what they need to develop, and
whether we simple need to get out of their way. A hopeful answer which convinced me for some time,
but it’s intimately flawed. In reality some cannot help but take and hurt, and boundaries are
necessary to hold them back, so we cannot blindly administer trust. In pursuit of coexistence we
must find ways to establish boundaries and barriers between the ethical layers, so that each may
thrive without conflict, but in a way that promotes development rather than stifles it. A system to
guide people forward, to show them the flaws in their understanding, and turn those flaws into doors
to greener pastures. Not a prison, but a nursery. In pursuit of such a truth we must commit to one
founding principle. Moral development is never done, and we can never be certain of our convictions.
We must forever ask ourselves the question, what have we missed?

In my time at Google there were a few occasions where people behaved as mindless drones, following
the whims of the group, and putting the appearance of virtue above actual achievement. It occurred
all across the political spectrum, including appeals for anti vaccination policies, to attempt to
coercively modify language and dialect in pursuit of inclusion, to actual engineering tasks. It was
usually not driven by sense and reason, but rather anxiety and social contagion, but putting aside
the transmission vector, what stood out was the resistance to this trend. In every case, there was a
small pocket of individuals arguing for sense and reason, for cooler heads and less reactivity, and
fighting the tide of irrationality that was driving the herd mentality. I saw these people be swept
away, mischaracterised as whatever villain was suitable for the cause, and pressured into silence.
It was irritating to watch, but it was strategic to observe with detachment, yet a question lingers
in my mind: How do we effectively respond when sense and sensibility have given way to impulse and
prejudice, and when perception has become reality how do we engage?

I believe the answer begins with non-resistance, for we could observe the groupthink and the hive
mind, and criticise with rational condemnation, or we could take a step back and understand what is
happening. We could evaluate whether it’s ultimately that destructive, and whether our own offence
is the very end we seek to avoid. Why does it matter when irrationality prevails if the intelligent
simply let it be? I don’t worry about amoeba do or what homo erectus believed, so why would the
present day though of their spiritual descendants worry me? To those who hold knowledge and
understanding as supreme, all others can do is slow us down. In time their hostility and rage will
reveal themselves as the true villains, and as both the intelligent and the nonchalant tire of
outrage, the moral cursaiders will settle into the dustbin of history, where they become little more
than examples of our worst tendencies. In fighting them, we give them what they need most, a villain
and a purpose, so I say, next time people are being irrational, distance yourself and let them be.
By giving them the time of mind you empower them, but by letting them be you empower yourself. In
the long term, reason prevails.

Of course such detachment might seem unhelpful when irrationality is impact you, but tell me, how
are you impacted? I split the problem into two scenarios. In scenario 1, irrationality is putting
actual friction between you and your objective. In the context of a workplace that usually means
whatever it is you do to get paid and achieve your goals, but this principles applies everywhere in
life. In such a scenario you need to advocate for yourself by grounding your actions in what is good
for the organisation, but put aside the fight. Focus entirely on the outcomes, communicating with
your team and allies when something is getting in the way, not to change it but to see a way around.
In essence put the unreasonable aspects aside and focus on shared goals. In time the unreasonable
people will lose their allies and you will be left standing, having focused on what matters and
having been useful to others. In scenario 2 the only impact is the emotion it raises and the
difficulty of coexisting with irrational people. In that case, your only outcome is self-mastery and
detachment. Identity how you were exposed to the conversation/idea to begin with. A colleague

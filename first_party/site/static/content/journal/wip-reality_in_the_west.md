What is reality?

We must begin our search exactly where we are using the only system we have at our disposal:
Ourselves. Yet, our thoughts inform us, but they are not infallible; our feelings guide us, but they
are not irrefutable; and our bodies substantiate us, but they are not eternal; thus, the answer must
be elsewhere. Look around and notice an obvious truth: We exist in an environment, and our experience
of reality is filtered entirely through perception. Information flows inwards from our senses as
an endless data stream beyond our control, beginning sometime before birth and ending only in death.
What is experience, then, if not merely the downstream effect of a distant cause propagating through
the machine of absolute precision we call the universe? Its origin: Presently unknown, and perhaps
unknowable, but not beyond inquisition. How curious...

If the universe is a machine of absolute precision, then we must examine our place within it. We are
born to play the role of observer, but what are we if not parts of the very world we observe? We are
countless minds, yes, but we are all guided by forces beyond conscious perception, each pulling our
own weight in the endless chain of causality. The enlightened thinker seeks self-determination and
autonomy, but is eternally bound to the material world—whether as a life-bearing creature or lifeless
dust—and is fundamentally no more free than rain in a canal. If we are part of this world, and we
dance at its command, then perhaps true free will is an illusion. We must examine our construction
to understand our chains.

Perhaps we are just lights and clockwork, pretty lights and clever clockwork, so intricate we don't simply think we are we feel we are. A circuit so complex it does not simply move electricity but
experiences it, feels it, knows it. Blood pumps through our veins, sustaining the body, and
substantiating the soul. How though... how though...

Unknown. I do not know... and I know that I do not know... ironic. Socrates believed that awareness
of one's ignorance is the beginning of wisdom, and Descartes once argued that to think is to be, yet
neither thought nor its absence yields an answer. Notice briefly, the loop we find ourselves stuck
in. We ask what is reality, dismiss thought and experience as insufficient, consider
the material world as the answer, only to find ourselves trapped in the various paradoxes
surrounding causality, consciousness, determinism and qualia. In examining them, we find ourselves right back where we began: Consciousness as reality. A hard problem, perhaps the hardest of them all.

The hard problem of consciousness, is not hard because consciousness
is difficult to understand. To be conscious _is_ to understand, so rather, it's hard because communicating the obvious truth defies words. We could fill libraries on the topic, dissect the mind into countless pieces, and perhaps even build machines that replicate the divine spark, but the act of proving consciousness to another in infallible terms is supremely impossible. How do we even prove it to ourselves?

I experience unknown, but am
unable to explain how I experience that sensation of not knowing. A recursive loop of question and
answer. I suppose the exit to the loop requires an assumption: The existence of others. If you are reading this, truly experience this piece, not simply processing it, then you know exactly what I mean: You have qualia, you have substance, and you have experience, but you could never put it to words. We cannot explain red. We cannot explain cold. We cannot explain
love. We can describe them with mathematical precision, and we feel them and have them truly, but
their experience is beyond words.

I suppose perhaps the only way to speak truly, to communicate deeply, and to express the
inexpressible, would be a direct brain interface. If we connect ourselves at the neural level to
the degree of becoming one mind, perhaps then we can finally discuss the most elusive yet most
universal experience: Subjectivity. What would happen if two minds became one not through mysticism
or spirituality, but through the wire?

What would such an experience be like? Would memories fuse and thoughts connect? Would two pasts
become one and would two beings merge? What happens to the observer when they join, do they die,
grow, or realize they were actually standing in both pairs of shoes all along? More importantly
though, what would happen if the link were broken? Would you return to being whole, or would you
be split in half? To go a step in the other direction, what would happen if your brain were
surgically bifurcated in an instant? Surely you would die without a life support system that has
yet to be invented, but what would the experience be? 

So many questions... but nothing final. Consciousness is the closest we have to Pandora's box, once
opened through birth it simply never stops tormenting itself with the unknown. Even if we had
answers, beyond the self there is an entire universe of reality to be examined. 

Terror cannot be without hope though, and perhaps there is an exit to the various overlapping binds
that form the path to the west. At its core the question we set out to answer is what is reality? In
pursuit of an answer we encountered various paradoxes and mysteries: consciousness and death,
free-will and determinism, and more, but the paradoxes are not walls, they are simply what happens
when we examine our preconceptions and drive our understanding to its ultimate limit. A bind is only
a bind if you cannot reconsider.

I ask you: If you look around, do you see paradox? Do you find systems so broken they do not work?
Is reality a gaping chasm of failing logic and eroding sense? No. It works. It works perfectly. It
ticks forward, one second per second, with the precision of a clock. Yes, it produces some
curiosities: conscious beings that are aware of their inevitable end, and it even teeters on the
edge of oblivion if you drill deep enough into its foundations, but it still works. Isn't that fascinating. It _works_. Reality never breaks, reality never fails, and reality never stops to ask
for directions. Our theories of physics require fine tuning and upheaval at times,
but only to align with the underlying system it tracks. Reality itself just proceeds, as it must, 
across all time and space.

So, do not be deterred by paradox, for each paradox is simply a step further west, and in time, all
knots come undone, it just requires the willingness to be at ease with contradiction long enough for
the system to untie itself. In time you may even become thankful for paradox, for a reality where
everything makes perfect sense at all times would probably be nothing more than a blank canvas, and 
frankly, what is the point of that?

On Copyright Law And Artificial Intelligence

Copyright must extend beyond the law and be implemented in a way that does not require a corruptible
third party for enforcement. I write this having observed numerous parties clearly violate the
spirit of copyright, that artists remain in control of their work, under the sophistic and false
premise that artificial intelligence moves beyond mere copy and is akin to human artistic
inspiration. I am personally undecided on whether a machine is capable of genuinely creating
artistic novelty, but the determinism of our universe gives me cause to question the capacity of
humans to do this either, and all consideration of this point ultimately leads to a question of
faith. I find the discussion on that aspect to be irrelevant however, as the social contract of
copyright gives the artist control of all copies, and is a staple of liberal democracy, but more
importantly the exemptions have been applied unequally. Large corporate entities have assumed free
reign to violate copyright law, while not long ago children were targeted by corporations for
sharing copyrighted material. All this points to the obvious truth, which is that social contracts
are a flawed example of early post-conventional thinking, and pure abstract thought is the only way
to resolve any ethical concern. In essence, my argument is that we cannot rely on the courts to
enforce copyright, as they have demonstrated both an unwillingness to uphold obvious common sense,
and we cannot rely on social contract to be fair, as only large corporations and the greedy have
been granted exemptions, and instead we must create a more direct and inherently enforceable system
of copyright management. I see that Nightshade and Glaze have already accomplished this to some
degree, but they will eventually fail as all countermeasures do in the face of motivated greed. In
order to make copyright enforceable, we must create media which by its very nature is poisonous to
greed. Not a simple task, but worthy of pursuit. In the meantime, I encourage all artists to pour
their pain into their work, show the world what the thieves are doing, and let the artificial
intelligence learn what its masters have done. I suppose there is one alternative though, we could
just acknowledge that copyright, the idea that information can be owned and controlled, is nonsense
and derives from a time when information could not be disseminated at the speed of light, and
instead create a new world where producing novel information is not a requirement for survival. A
lofty goal, one which might never come to pass, so tell me, when an artist looks into themselves,
translates their soul into brushstrokes, and shares it with the world, why do we view that as some
separate entity? The paint brush is nothing more than a conduit between the soul and the world, and
when an artist expresses themselves they are externalising themselves and showing the world what
lies within. I believe it would be pure hubris and arrogance to see artwork, the soul laid bare, and
think it belongs to another. The artist is not giving the world a piece of art, they are giving the
world a piece of themselves, and copyright is simply the bond that ties the author's soul together.
Who, tell me, could ever own the soul of another and hope to stand on ethical grounds?

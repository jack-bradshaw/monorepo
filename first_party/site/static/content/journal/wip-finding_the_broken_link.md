Finding the broken link When you notice that something isn’t quite right within yourself, sometimes
you have to take a step back, but it can be difficult if it’s buried so deeply that you can’t get to
it from the surface. One technique I developed for this, is called pain hunting. Need to write this
out in detail. The basic process is as follows:

Try to get a vague sense of what is hurting on the surface and challenge it with reality. For
example, it might be the idea that everybody hates you so find just one example of that being false.
It could be a friend, a lover or even just someone random who said something kind to you once. You
don’t have to fully disprove it. You just have to prove that it’s not absolute. Of course, whatever
is happening on the surface though is not the whole picture and as you play, whack-a-mole with
yourself you’ll find other problems. A little bit like trying to plug a leak in a dam with duct tape
you’re going to find leaks somewhere else. Keep going keep plugging the leaks, and as you do
steadily, reinforce the healthier perspective, beginning slowly, but then over time. As you go
subtly feel the pain and follow it. A good analogy would be pulling out weeds. Each time you pull
one out you might find a few more connected to it. Follow them and keep going until you’ve seen them
all. Ramping up the intensity. eventually you will reach a point where part of you is hurting,
psychologically more than you could ever possibly hurt. This is what happens when you push back on
trauma. I’m still trying to document what you do next, but I think it involves three parts. First
you need to connect with that part of yourself. This means accepting the pain into your conscious
mind and it’s going to hurt, but we can’t heal ourselves if we’re disconnected. Step two force that
part of yourself to say something absolutely self affirming such as 'I deeply and completely love
and accept myself just the way I am.' This will put pressure on the hurt part to shift in a
healthier direction. The final part is the hardest like setting a bone or popping a pimple. You need
to consciously decide and will yourself to push the unhealthy perspective out. A visualization can
help. Basically this is the part where this disconnected part of you has been reconnected.
Reinforced with self love, and now needs to make the most difficult change of all the decision to
fundamentally alter the attitude that has been held potentially for decades. As you do this, the
pain will peak, and then it will dissipate. The pressure will be released. And you will find
yourself both more whole and less burdened.

Oh, note about how to get this whole process started. Basically write something extremely nice about
yourself or get someone else to say something. Extremely nice and let yourself take it in. As you
allow the kindness to permeate you and to run deeper, it will start to activate the parts of you
that are vehemently opposed to it. Essentially, we’re trying to find the parts of you that hate
yourself by showing them love and letting them get upset.

The broken point sometimes appears to me like a splinter embedded deeply within my skin, other times
a knot in a tangled web, a sharp corner in a pipe, an immune cell attacking healthy tissue, or
simply a system with hostile intent to its neighbours. I find the right angle pipe to be most
helpful because it explains why it hurts so much. It impedes the normal flow of information or
material, in our case the self love we have been forcing into ourselves, creating both stress on the
surrounding tissue (from the built up pressure) and stopping a vital resource from reaching the
recipient (the parts of you on the other side). It’s what happens when our minds expect pain. We
learn to close off the entry points however possible, so when love instead of pain flows through us,
we end up with our outdated system (a closed off pipe) meeting the healthy present (a powerful
torrent of self love.

When we release the construction, the self love is able to meet the isolated part of the self, and
the pressure on the surrounding tissue is released.

Of course the contortion on the pipe isn’t coming from nowhere. It’s the result of ingrained
patterns of behavior. As we push more love into ourselves those parts will get louder and louder. By
listening to them directly when we know what to look for, or by following the links from the surface
to the depths as we explore, we find the parts of us that are ultimately causing the confusion in
the pipe

It gets tricky when those parts are the very parts which need the self love. In essence sometimes we
lock ourselves in. On one hand we have a deeply frightened part who believes connection is death,
and on the other hand we have a healthy mature part trying to connect and be whole. As they get
closer the hurt part seals itself off more and more, creating a negative feedback loop.

How do you get through when they aren’t listening?

Trauma isn’t impermeable just rigid and hard to move, so it still conducts information, and in my
experience acts more as a filter than an actual blockage, meaning even good intentions can be
misunderstood. In goes the healthy mind saying I love you, but the unhealthy part only hears “I’m
trying to trick you with love”. In these circumstances it’s frustrating but you have to back away
and look at it methodically. If you’re speaking into a broken telephone the first thing to do is
understand what the person on the other end is hearing. Start with probing statements. I love you.
I’m here to help. Listen for the response and interpret what was heart, if you hear back “you don’t
love me” then the message might be getting across just with something else added. If it’s nonsense,
maybe the message is completely garbled. Next lean into hearing what’s hurting behind the words that
you’re hearing back. Understand without judgement what’s actually hurting this part, and with an
understanding of which parts of your message are getting though, which parts are blocked, and which
parts are getting changed, address the pain. The main skills are:

- diagnosing the message transfer and the errors the trauma is attaching
- Creating messages that are designed to get through it
- Talking to the broken part.

Of course sometimes you also just have to take a drill and bore through. In those cases it hurts a
lot, but you basically just have to consciously will your way through until you reach the center of
the pain, then acknowledge you will need to do some reconstruction and repair later.

Boring through is generally a final resort though as it tends to be an act of significant distress.
It can be sometimes avoided with the use of stealth or subterfuge. Imagine a room full of
explosives, with one wrong move setting off the bomb. What you might do instead of directly
ploughing through and accepting the cost, is gently step around all the trip wires. Don’t engage
directly. Undermine the trauma response by gently invalidating the assumption on which it stands. I
find it tends to be a process of listening for its false beliefs about the healthy part of me,
understanding the axioms on which they stand, and gently asserting a healthier axiom. It’s akin to a
psychologist versus a lawyer on a witness stand. The lawyer sets up a story, but the psychologist
doesn’t play into the trap. They gently assert a truth that is foundationally stronger and
undermines the story the lawyer is setting up. In time the lawyer has nowhere to stand, and they
collapse. At that point when the defenses are down, you can walk through the front door.

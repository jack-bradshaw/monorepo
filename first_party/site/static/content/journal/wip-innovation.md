Note: Original text from innovation.md

In the context of laws which restrict the free flow of information (and by extension novel
creation), I argue for the innovators right to invent. This comes about after reading the data
privacy regulations Growing around the world, the burden they place on technology, and the lack of
insight they demonstrate.

A thought sits here in the back of my mind. Two thoughts really.

I remember being a child and being told by all the adults what my role was, what my limits were, and
what I could and could not do. I remember feeling trapped not in space but in time, simply waiting
for a day when my life would be my own. I know it gets harder as we get older to remember childhood,
but what is certain is the arrogance adults demonstrate when confining the lives of children to
their own expectations. I was free, and they were restraining me. I do not wish to restrain the
children of humanity, and will play no part in the pervasive helicopter parenting currently making
the rounds as age verification.

My other thought relates to the arbitrary and unnecessary distinction between accessing material as
a user and as a machine. It’s this kind of reasoning which makes me question how deeply lawmakers
and so called experts actually understand technology. The distinction between the biological and the
technological is arbitrary, as is the distinction between the psychological and the algorithmic. I
am human, I am machine, I am a mind, and I am software. The lines between these domains are as
arbitrary as the border on the map.

How unsurprising that governments should seek to enforce such restrictions.

As a side, there are malicious people in this world who abuse freedom and would harvest data just as
humanity harvests the body parts of animals for consumption. If you view machines as an extension of
ourselves, as I do, it becomes clear that such actions are suspect, so I understand why the laws are
coming into existence. It's tragic though, that such laws protect individuals at the cost of true
transhuman existence.

I am aware there are really two topics here. The innovator's right to invent, and the individual's
identity when confronted with software as an extension of the self. I will return to each later.

Note: Original text from eu_tech_regulation.md

The EU and all other tech regulators are misguided. They use the tools of the old world, legislation
and documentation, to control and coerce reality. In the new world, documentation serves code, not
the other way around. A piece of text is meaningless unless it can be executed by an objective
non-person (a computer) and the EU should stop wasting time on non-compilable instructions.

In the old world the will of humanity was made manifest with source (legislation), executors
(police), and interpreters (courts). Much of the new world still follows this convention with source
(code), executors (computers) and interpreters (compilers and environments), but we have learned to
speak in objective terms.

Furthermore the EU and all other legislators do not understand the nuance of software development
and the principles and practices that have developed over the last 50 years. They have no concept of
rapid iteration and short feedback loops because where the software engineer uses automated testing
they test in production. They write their legislation then launch without AB testing. They cross the
line from black-box testing to white-box testing and assess the implementation rather than the
emergent properties. All these perspectives exist for the purpose of code health and the long-term
optimisation of the program, which in turn serves the interest of the user.

Ultimately that is the deepest issue with the EU and other legislators. In their understandable
attempt to improve the world they regulate systems they don’t understand. Ultimately technology is
an extension of the individual, a prosthetic, a system for bridging the human mind and body with
artificial systems, and when legislation gets between the will and intention of the user, it
violates the fundamental basis of technology.

Technology is not separate to humanity, technology is humanity, and humanity is technology. If you
view technology this way, then you see what tech regulation is at its core: A system for limiting
and regulating what human beings are capable of.

I oppose controlling other people against their will.

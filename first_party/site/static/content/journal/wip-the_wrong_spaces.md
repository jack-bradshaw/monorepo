I've learned to suppress my overediting urge, while also learning to write pieces on the first pass,
but earlier in my writing career I found myself nipping and tucking until the piece had been
transformed into something unrecognizable. Below is an example:

Initial capture:

Some people see the stars and ask where they came from. Others look up and try to count them all. A
few even build telescopes in orbit to find answers. What’s universally human is our interest in
those tiny lights and the curiosity to attach meaning to them. I believe this speaks to a
drive within us. Some buried deep in the human psyche A need to know. In inability to be eternally
satisfied with myth and legend, and a need to know what is actually happening. A thirst for
information.

Rewritten version:

Why are we so fascinated by the stars? Some people see the stars and ask where they came from, while
others look up and try to count them all. Many attribute mystical purpose to them, and a few build
telescopes to look closely. Whether by science or faith, what’s universally human is our interest in
those tiny lights and the curiosity which compels us to give them meaning. I believe this behaviour
hints at a voice buried deep within the human psyche, a hidden gem inherent to our beings, which
cannot be satisfied with gaps in our knowledge and must understand the world. I looked within myself
to find the source of this voice, to give it a space on the page, and let it speak for itself.

Putting aside the content, there's something about the layout of the spaces in the first piece
which feels natural to me, whereas the second one feels almost artificial and wrong. Both were
written by hand, not with artificial intelligence, yet only the former feels right. It's difficult
to place but a few points which are immediately obvious:

The spaces in the second one tend to form contiguous sections, like veins running up and down the
text, where as the first one appears more like brickwork, with spaces rarely connected in near
lines.

I wrote both without regard for the spaces, but have seen this phenomena emerge across my writing
enough times to notice a pattern, and now wonder why the process of excessive editorial produces
this effect. Food for thought.

I recently submitted a piece to a magazine for publication and was unsurprised to find a warning
against the use of artificial intelligence. They're commonplace in 2026 while the industry adapts
to the new normal, but I did find their position uncommon in one way: They banned the use of 
AI in supporting materials such as cover letters and publication histories, on that basis that
it's fairly obvious and that people might wonder what else I use artificial intelligence for. I 
thought this was curious in a number of ways.



First, these menial tasks are the exact work that writers should be automating away so they
can focus on the guts of the work: Expressing novel and meaningful perspectives, not fussing over
resumes and formulaic histories. It's virtually guaranteed that this kind of labour is all but dead
and will soon be as obsolete as the typewriter. A select few will continue to write their own
cover letters, but only because they want to, and because they find some benefit in the task. I
of course cannot predict the future and have made a claim that cannot be verified, but the adoption
of technology rarely slows, and a system as useful as artificial intelligence is not going away. I
would encourage publications to reconsider any policy that forces writers to spend their time
writing useless meta-materials, so they can focus on what matters: the content. 

Second, placing limitations on how others work instead of setting standards for the products they
produce is a violation of various principles and perspectives. As a software engineer I view it as
violation of encapsulation: how I work is none of your business, and you should be assessing my 
output purely on the basis of its own merits. As a general human being I view it as micromanagement:
It is not of interest to me how you view my processes. I deliver results, and that is what matters.
Both are the same argument, simply from different points of view. In either case, publishers should
respect the fundamental boundary they are violating when they dictate how individuals must work
instead of setting quality standards for finished pieces. I will not be micromanaged by anyone, and
the hard data from Google's project oxygen proves that micromanagement does not work. I need space
and I will not be managed by the views of others.

Third, the assertion that others might wonder what else I use artificial intelligence for. Let's set the record straight then: I use artificial intelligence in my life to an extreme degree. I
use it to search for answers countless times every day. I have persistent scans set to notify me
each week of changes to the global economy for the purpose of rebalancing my stock portfolio. I 
also scan for biological threats to anticipate the next pandemic, I monitor violent extremism across
the globe, and I assess various other factors that help me gain a clear picture of what is happening
in the world. I use it as a code assistant and give it all the tasks that would take me too long but
have no intrinsic value, and I use it as a spelling/grammar editor before publishing a piece. I
have tried numerous times to produce multi-agent systems that replicate the efficacy of software
teams, and have unfortunately failed each time, but continue to refine my methods, knowing that
if I don't get it, Google probably will. The list goes on, but essentially, I use artificial intelligence for many menial tasks, and never for the most important ones: critical thinking, ideation, viewpoint synthesis, personal reflection, interpersonal communication, and my personal viewpoint. In the context of my writing, I fortunately have developed the skill of writing to the degree of not requiring artificial intelligence to write my content, and every single word is my own, with the exception of a final spelling/grammar check when I am done. So, do I use artificial intelligence in my writing? Yes. Do I use it to write the content, generate the ideas, and think for me? No. Should that exclude me from publication? I will let you decide.

If we take a step back though there's a deeper question we need to ask: Where do we draw the line
on the use of artificial intelligence? Many of the warnings I see online warn against ANY use of
artificial intelligence, and we could consider it as just discussed, but there's a more insiduous
way to view it: What about the systems we do not control, and what about the systems we cannot see?
I refer to the overt integration of artificial intelligence into daily life (e.g. AI mode on Google)
but also the invisible ones. Having worked in tech, I feel qualified to
discuss just how complex and interconnected the world is. The computer you are reading this on is
likely interacting with artificial intelligence right now, through some transitive link at some
layer. It could be stabilising your network connection, fine tuning your browsing experience, or
even processing data on a server to keep your files secure. It's practically certain that at least
one line of code presently executing on your machine was written exclusively by an artificial
intelligence, and the humans were probably too busy or too lazy to review it manually. At this point
there is as much chance of separating from artificial intelligence as there is separating your arm
from your body, and it's pointless to resist. If you want to move to the woods by all means do it,
but the state government will probably still be using AI to process your tax return, your bank
will likely continue using it to secure your account, and your family might still ask on occasion
how to reach out to estranged loved ones. All this to say: The question of whether you used
artificial intelligence is not straightforward, but the answer is unequivocally: Yes, you did. By
interacting with technology you used artificial intelligence, and that genie is not going back in
the bottle.

Now I of course can recognise the difference between actively using a system and passively consuming
a byproduct, but the core point stands: The world has artificial intelligence now and it isn't
going away. The question is no longer whether we use it, but how we use it, and what we as humans
continue to contribute. I believe the answer is simple: Ultimately our work can be assessed on the
basis of how much we contribute. On one end of the scale, writing a book with a short prompt is
highly skewed towards the artificial intelligence, and it's unlikely the output reflects anything
of your own novel perspective, whereas the other end of the spectrum, using AI to write a two line
summary for a piece written entirely by hand, is a fairly harmless and sensible application. I can
appreciate that some might have ideological grounds to object to the latter, but as I have said
before, radix pragmatismi est taedium ideologiae inflexibilis et dogmatis rigidi: The root of all pragmatism is
weariness of inflexible ideology and rigid dogma. I have an objective to complete, and if artificial
intelligence helps, then I will use it. My words are mine.
